Dementia Prevention: 8 Evidence-Based Strategies

Roughly 40% of dementia cases may be linked to factors within your control. Here are the eight strategies with the strongest evidence for protecting your brain as you age.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published July 17, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Not inevitable: the Lancet Commission estimates up to 40–45% of dementia cases are linked to modifiable risk factors.
  • Physical activity has the most consistent, replicated evidence of any lifestyle habit for reducing dementia risk.
  • Sleep clears the brain's waste: the glymphatic system flushes amyloid and tau proteins during deep sleep each night.
  • Cardiovascular and brain health are linked : what damages your heart's vessels often damages the brain's too.
  • Start now: midlife habits may carry the greatest long-term impact, but protective steps are worth taking at any age.

Dementia is not an inevitable part of aging. A landmark series of reviews in The Lancet estimates that roughly 40–45% of dementia cases worldwide may be linked to modifiable risk factors, meaning conditions and habits we have some ability to change. Genetics still play a role, and no strategy guarantees anything. But the eight evidence-based practices below give you real, actionable ways to lower your personal risk over time. If you're worried about memory changes in yourself or someone you love, talk with your doctor. This article is a starting point, not a substitute for medical advice.

1. Move your body every day

Among all lifestyle factors studied, regular physical activity has the most consistently replicated evidence for reducing dementia risk. Aerobic exercise increases blood flow to the hippocampus (the brain's memory hub), supports the growth of new neurons, and reduces chronic low-grade inflammation linked to cognitive decline. You don't need to run long distances. Research shows that brisk walking for around 30 minutes most days produces measurable effects on brain structure and function. Adding resistance training a couple of times a week may offer additional protection. Doing both consistently, over years, is where the benefit compounds. For more on the brain-body connection, see our guide to exercise and brain health.

2. Follow a brain-protective eating pattern

No single food prevents dementia. But overall eating patterns matter quite a bit. The Mediterranean diet and the MIND diet (a variation designed specifically for brain health) are both consistently associated with slower cognitive decline and lower dementia incidence in large, long-running population studies. Both emphasize vegetables, legumes, whole grains, fish, olive oil, berries, and nuts, while limiting red meat, butter, processed foods, and sweets. You don't have to follow either diet perfectly; even moderate adherence appears protective. Our guide to foods and daily habits for memory has a practical breakdown of what to eat and what to cut back on.

3. Protect your sleep

Sleep is when the brain clears waste. The glymphatic system, a fluid-driven clearance network that runs mainly during deep, slow-wave sleep, flushes out metabolic byproducts including the amyloid and tau proteins that accumulate in Alzheimer's disease. Consistently sleeping fewer than 7 hours a night, especially with poor sleep quality, is associated with greater buildup of those proteins over time. The practical implication is straightforward: keep a consistent sleep schedule, keep your bedroom dark and cool, and limit alcohol before bed (it fragments sleep even when it makes falling asleep easier). 4. Keep learning new things

Researchers use the term "cognitive reserve" to describe the brain's resilience to damage. People who spend their lives in mentally demanding work and activities tend to maintain sharper cognition longer, even when imaging shows the same physical changes seen in dementia. Learning a new language, taking up an instrument, mastering a new skill, reading challenging material. All of these appear to build that reserve. The key factor seems to be genuine challenge: activities need to push you outside your comfort zone and require active mental effort. Passive entertainment doesn't carry the same benefit. 5. Stay socially connected

Loneliness and social isolation are recognized risk factors for cognitive decline, and not minor ones. Regular, meaningful social engagement gives the brain a genuine workout: following a conversation, reading a room, recalling shared history, catching humor. These activities exercise attention, language, and memory all at once. The type of connection matters less than its consistency and depth. Group activities, close friendships, phone calls with family, and volunteering all appear protective in large cohort studies. If social isolation has crept into your life (a common pattern after retirement or major life changes), addressing it deliberately is worth the effort.

6. Take care of your cardiovascular health

Brain health and heart health are deeply connected. What damages blood vessels in the heart also damages the small vessels that supply brain tissue. High blood pressure in midlife is one of the most impactful modifiable risk factors on the Lancet Commission's list. Type 2 diabetes, high LDL cholesterol, and obesity each independently raise dementia risk through overlapping mechanisms: reduced cerebral blood flow, increased inflammation, and damage to white matter. Managing these conditions through diet, regular exercise, and medication where appropriate gives your brain a significantly better long-term outlook. If you haven't had your blood pressure or metabolic markers checked recently, that's a good first step to take.

7. Address hearing loss

Hearing loss may seem unrelated to dementia, but it appears consistently in research as one of the most impactful modifiable risk factors, particularly in midlife. One proposed explanation is that untreated hearing loss forces the brain to spend extra cognitive resources just to process sound, leaving less capacity for memory and other functions over time. Hearing aids and other interventions appear to help. If you've been noticing that you're asking people to repeat themselves, or turning the TV up, getting a hearing assessment is a straightforward and potentially important step.

8. Quit smoking and limit alcohol

Smoking damages blood vessels throughout the body, and those in the brain are no exception. Long-term smokers carry a meaningfully higher risk of dementia compared to non-smokers, and quitting reduces that risk over time, even later in life. Heavy alcohol use raises dementia risk through direct neurotoxic effects and by fragmenting the deep sleep the brain depends on. Moderate drinking appears far less damaging than heavy or binge patterns, and the evidence doesn't support light drinking as a protective strategy. If you smoke, quitting is one of the highest-impact changes you can make for your brain's long-term health. Talk to your doctor about cessation support options.

Dementia prevention isn't a single breakthrough habit. It's a set of everyday choices (sleep, movement, social connection, a heart-healthy diet) that protect brain tissue and build resilience over decades.

Frequently asked questions

Can dementia actually be prevented?

Not with certainty, but the Lancet Commission estimates that roughly 40–45% of dementia cases may be linked to modifiable risk factors. Addressing those factors meaningfully lowers risk over time. Speak with your doctor if you are concerned about memory changes.

What is the most important thing you can do to prevent dementia?

The evidence points to combining habits rather than relying on one fix. Physical activity, consistent sleep, and protecting cardiovascular health each have strong research behind them, and their effects compound when practiced together.

At what age should I start thinking about dementia prevention?

Research shows that brain changes linked to dementia can begin two decades or more before symptoms appear. Starting protective habits in midlife (your 40s and 50s) may carry the greatest long-term impact, but the evidence suggests real benefit at any age.

Does diet help prevent dementia?

No single food prevents dementia, but eating patterns similar to the Mediterranean or MIND diet are consistently linked to slower cognitive decline in large population studies. They are not a guarantee, but they are among the best-studied protective habits.
