Anxiety and Forgetfulness: The Overlooked Link

When anxiety is chronic, forgetfulness is rarely far behind. Elevated cortisol impairs the hippocampus; persistent worried thoughts crowd out working memory. Here's what the research shows and what helps.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Today

Key takeaways

  • Cortisol is the mechanism. Anxiety activates the stress response, and chronically elevated cortisol directly impairs the hippocampus where new memories form.
  • Worried thoughts consume working memory. The mental bandwidth anxiety takes up leaves less room for everyday information to register.
  • Forgetfulness is a recognized symptom of generalized anxiety disorder, not just a side effect of feeling stressed.
  • Treating the anxiety typically improves memory alongside mood, not as a separate task.
  • Exercise, sleep, and targeted nutrients help break the cortisol-memory cycle and support both conditions together.

Most people recognize anxiety as a feeling: the racing thoughts, the tight chest, the sense that something is about to go wrong. Fewer realize it also systematically degrades memory. Two distinct biological mechanisms connect them, and understanding both explains why forgetfulness so often trails persistent anxiety, and why fixing the anxiety tends to fix the forgetfulness too.

What does cortisol do to memory?

Cortisol is the body's primary stress hormone. In short bursts, it's useful. It sharpens attention and primes you for fast decisions. The problem is chronic elevation. Sustained high cortisol inhibits a process called long-term potentiation, which is the cellular mechanism the hippocampus uses to form new memories. Over months and years, it can reduce hippocampal volume outright.

Multiple studies in people with untreated anxiety disorders show measurably smaller hippocampi compared to matched controls without anxiety. This isn't just a biological footnote. The everyday result is difficulty encoding new memories: conversations that fade quickly, names that simply don't register, items you were certain you'd remember but didn't.

This same cortisol-hippocampus connection shows up in chronic stress, which is why the two conditions so often look alike from the outside. For a deeper look at how stress specifically disrupts recall, our guide on stress and memory covers the mechanism in full.

Why worried thoughts make you forgetful

The second mechanism is more immediate. Anxious thinking is intrusive and persistent. Those loops of "what if" occupy working memory, the cognitive workspace you use for everything from holding a phone number while dialing to following a conversation to remembering why you walked into a room.

Working memory has limited capacity. Research consistently shows that emotionally loaded or threatening thoughts get prioritized automatically, whether you intend it or not. The practical result: mundane, everyday information gets crowded out. You were technically present. You just weren't paying attention in the way encoding requires.

One study published in Cognitive Therapy and Research found that people with high anxiety showed significant working memory impairment on tasks requiring sustained attention, specifically because intrusive thoughts interrupted the maintenance of information. The anxiety itself was the interference, not a side effect of it.

What types of memory does anxiety affect most?

Working memory takes the biggest hit because it depends so directly on sustained attention. Episodic memory (the kind that lets you recall specific events and conversations) is also vulnerable, since encoding those memories requires the focused attention anxiety tends to divert elsewhere.

Procedural memory, the type that governs how to drive, type, or play an instrument, is more resistant. This is why performance anxiety affects newer, less practiced skills much more than deeply ingrained ones. The skills that run on "autopilot" are largely insulated from the effects of cortisol and working memory load.

How do you know if anxiety is behind the forgetfulness?

A few patterns point to anxiety as the driver rather than aging or another cause. The forgetfulness tends to worsen during periods of heightened worry. You find you were present for conversations but can't recall what was said minutes later. Sleep problems accompany the forgetfulness, because anxiety disrupts the deep sleep that memory consolidation depends on. (See sleep and memory for how that cycle works.)

Other anxiety-specific signs: restlessness, difficulty concentrating on tasks you normally handle without effort, and a persistent sense that your mind is "somewhere else." What actually helps?

Addressing the anxiety directly is the most reliable path to reducing the forgetfulness alongside it. Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) has the strongest evidence base for generalized anxiety, and clinical studies consistently show it improves cognitive function alongside mood. When the mental noise quiets, recall typically follows.

Regular aerobic exercise is one of the most effective, accessible interventions for both anxiety and memory. It reduces cortisol, raises brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF, a protein that supports neuron health and hippocampal function), and meaningfully improves sleep quality. A brisk 20–30 minute walk most days is enough to produce measurable effects within weeks.

Mindfulness practices show moderate, consistent evidence for both anxiety reduction and working memory. The mechanism is essentially cognitive: reducing intrusive thoughts frees up the mental bandwidth that memory encoding depends on. Even brief daily sessions of 10–15 minutes show measurable improvements in some controlled trials.

Can nutrients help break the cycle?

Some address both mood and cognition at the same time. Magnesium supports the GABA pathways that calm the nervous system; deficiency is common in people with anxiety and can show up as difficulty switching off mentally or restless sleep. B-vitamins, particularly B6, are precursors to serotonin and GABA. Omega-3 fatty acids, especially DHA, have evidence for both cognitive function and mood regulation.

These aren't a replacement for treating an anxiety disorder, and they won't resolve significant anxiety on their own. But for everyday anxiety and stress-related forgetfulness, closing nutritional gaps is worth addressing alongside behavioral strategies. Frequently asked questions

Can anxiety cause memory problems?

Yes. Chronic anxiety raises cortisol, which impairs hippocampal function and inhibits new memory formation. Intrusive worried thoughts also consume working memory bandwidth, leaving less room for everyday information to stick. Forgetfulness is a recognized symptom of generalized anxiety disorder.

Why does anxiety make you forgetful?

Two mechanisms are at work: elevated cortisol directly impairs the hippocampus (where new memories form), and anxious, intrusive thoughts consume working memory capacity, leaving less room for routine information to register and stick.

Does treating anxiety improve memory?

In most cases, yes. CBT for anxiety consistently shows improvements in cognitive function alongside mood. When the mental noise reduces, recall typically sharpens as well.

What can I do today to help both anxiety and memory?

Regular aerobic exercise, consistent sleep, mindfulness practice, and closing nutrient gaps (magnesium, B-vitamins, omega-3) all support both conditions. If anxiety is significantly affecting daily life, talk to a healthcare provider first. These strategies work best alongside, not instead of, professional care.
