Is There a 'Bill Gates Brain Pill'? The Truth Behind the Viral Ads

You may have seen a slick video where Bill Gates "reveals" a brain pill that reverses memory loss. Here's what's really going on — and why you should close that tab.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published June 23, 2026

Key takeaways

  • No — Bill Gates does not sell or endorse any brain pill, memory supplement, or Alzheimer's "cure."
  • The viral ads are scams that use AI deepfakes of his face and voice to fake an endorsement.
  • Fact-checked and debunked by Snopes; the Gates Foundation warns scammers misuse its name; the FTC warns about bogus celebrity testimonials.
  • Red flags: miracle cure, "celebrity endorsement" with no trace on real accounts, fake news pages, AI voices.
  • What Gates actually does: funds Alzheimer's research — not pills.

No — Bill Gates does not sell or endorse any brain pill, memory supplement, or Alzheimer's "cure." If you've stumbled on a dramatic video or article where he supposedly unveils a "miracle" memory tablet, you are looking at a scam. These campaigns hijack his name, his face and even his voice to sell unproven supplements to people who are worried about their memory. Below we'll walk through exactly how the scam works, how to spot it, and what genuinely helps your brain instead.

So is there really a Bill Gates brain pill?

There isn't. Bill Gates has never created, branded, or recommended a memory supplement of any kind. The claim is fabricated. Fact-checking outlet Snopes has investigated and debunked viral ads tying celebrities to "brain pills," and the Gates Foundation has publicly warned that scammers misuse its name and Bill Gates's likeness to defraud people. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has also repeatedly warned consumers about bogus celebrity testimonials used to push health products. In other words: the people most associated with Gates's real work have all said, plainly, that these ads are fakes.

How does the scam actually work?

Once you've seen one of these campaigns, you'll recognize the pattern everywhere. It almost always runs in three stages:

1. The shocking ad. A social-media or video ad shows a famous, trusted figure "revealing" a breakthrough — sometimes a leaked interview, sometimes a tearful confession. The hook is emotional and urgent: a cure for a frightening disease, available for a limited time.

2. The fake "news" page. Click through and you land on a page dressed up to look like a real news article, TV interview transcript, or "documentary." It uses real network logos and a journalist-style byline, but none of it is genuine. It exists only to make the pitch feel credible.

3. The long video pitch (VSL). Finally you reach a long, hard-to-skip video that sells an unproven supplement, leaning on the fake celebrity "endorsement" the whole way. By the end you're pushed to buy multiple bottles immediately before the "offer expires."

Why do these ads look so convincing now?

The newest versions use AI deepfakes. Scammers feed real footage of Gates into AI tools to generate video where his lips move to a script he never said, paired with a cloned version of his voice. A few years ago a fake endorsement was easy to laugh off; today the production can look polished enough to fool a casual viewer. That's exactly why knowing the red flags matters more than ever.

If a billionaire had a pill that cured memory loss, it would be front-page news worldwide — not a skippable ad in your feed selling you three bottles before midnight.

What are the red flags of a fake celebrity supplement ad?

  • A miracle cure for a serious disease. Real medicine for conditions like Alzheimer's is hard, incremental, and heavily regulated — not a single over-the-counter pill.
  • An "endorsement" with zero trace on real accounts. If Gates truly backed a product, it would appear on his verified profiles and in mainstream coverage. Check. It won't be there.
  • Fake news-style pages. Logos and layouts that imitate real outlets, but with no working links, no real author, and a "buy now" button as the only real destination.
  • AI-sounding voices or odd visuals. Slightly off lip-sync, robotic cadence, or mismatched lighting are classic deepfake tells.
  • High-pressure urgency. Countdown timers, "only a few left," and steep discounts that vanish if you leave.

You'll see these same tactics attached to various pills marketed under names like "Brain Honey" or "Mind Boost" — generic products that ride on a borrowed famous face. When in doubt, search the product name plus the word "scam" and check Snopes or the FTC before you ever enter a card number.

What does Bill Gates actually do about Alzheimer's?

Here's the genuinely hopeful part the scammers exploit: Gates is involved in brain health — just not with pills. He has personally funded and championed Alzheimer's research, backing early detection, data sharing and new approaches to treatment. We cover this in detail in our explainer on Bill Gates and Alzheimer's research. That real work is precisely what makes the fake ads believable — they borrow the credibility of legitimate science to sell snake oil.

If there's no magic pill, what actually helps my memory?

This is the honest pivot. There is no celebrity-endorsed shortcut, but plenty of real, unglamorous things support memory and focus: consistent sleep, regular exercise, a brain-healthy diet, managing stress, and — when diet falls short — clinically-studied nutrients. What should I do if I've already seen (or bought from) one of these ads?

If you only watched the video, you've lost nothing but time. If you entered payment details, contact your bank or card issuer right away to dispute the charge and watch for recurring "subscription" billing, a common trick. You can also report the scam to the FTC. And if a product arrived, don't take it on the strength of a fake endorsement — talk to your doctor or pharmacist first.

Did Bill Gates create a brain or memory pill?

No. Bill Gates has never created, sold, or endorsed any brain pill, memory supplement, or Alzheimer's cure. Any ad claiming otherwise misuses his name and likeness and is a scam.
