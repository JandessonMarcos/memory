Prevagen vs Neuriva: Which Is Worth It in 2026?

They dominate the pharmacy shelf and the TV ads. But when you look past the marketing and into the actual ingredients and research, neither is what it appears to be. Here's the honest comparison.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published July 7, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Prevagen's main ingredient is apoaequorin, a protein derived from jellyfish. Its ability to cross the blood-brain barrier and improve memory is disputed by independent researchers.
  • Neuriva's two ingredients are phosphatidylserine (100 mg) and NeuroFactor coffee fruit extract (100 mg). The PS has better independent backing; the coffee fruit data is thin.
  • Neither formula is comprehensive. Both rely on one or two ingredients, missing key brain nutrients like citicoline, bacopa monnieri, and B-vitamins.
  • FTC scrutiny: Quincy Bioscience (maker of Prevagen) settled with the FTC in 2023 after years of action over its efficacy claims.
  • Cost vs. value: both run $30–$60 per month; more complete formulas offer a wider ingredient profile for comparable prices.

Walk into any pharmacy in America and these two bottles are hard to miss. Prevagen and Neuriva have built dominant positions in the over-the-counter memory supplement aisle through heavy advertising, recognizable packaging, and widespread availability. Millions of people buy them every year. Whether that's money well spent is a different question entirely, and the answer starts with looking at what's actually inside each bottle.

What's in Prevagen?

Prevagen's primary active ingredient is apoaequorin, a calcium-binding protein originally isolated from the jellyfish Aequorea victoria. Each regular-strength capsule contains 10 mg. There's also a small amount of vitamin D (50 mcg) in some formulations.

Quincy Bioscience, the company behind Prevagen, claims this protein is absorbed from the gut, enters the bloodstream, crosses the blood-brain barrier, and then improves cognitive function. Each step of that chain has been questioned by independent scientists. Proteins taken orally are typically broken down into amino acids during digestion, meaning the intact apoaequorin molecule wouldn't normally survive long enough to reach the brain. The company maintains it has evidence that some of the protein does get absorbed, but the independent data on this is sparse.

Prevagen comes in regular strength (10 mg), extra strength (20 mg), and professional strength (40 mg), at escalating price points. The difference in benefit at these levels has not been demonstrated in well-designed clinical trials.

What's in Neuriva?

Neuriva's formula is more transparent and relies on two ingredients: phosphatidylserine (PS) at 100 mg and NeuroFactor (a patented coffee fruit extract) at 100 mg. That's it. No other active brain nutrients appear on the label.

Phosphatidylserine is one of the better-researched brain supplements on the market. It's a phospholipid that makes up a significant portion of your neuron cell membranes. Several clinical trials have found that PS supplementation can support memory and cognitive function in older adults, and the FDA has granted it a qualified health claim related to reducing the risk of cognitive dysfunction. The doses used in most positive studies run 100–300 mg per day, so Neuriva's 100 mg is at the low end but not meaningless.

NeuroFactor is an extract from the whole coffee fruit (as opposed to the coffee bean itself) marketed for its ability to raise brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), a protein involved in neuron health. A few small studies do show BDNF increases after consuming whole coffee fruit extract. Translating higher BDNF to measurable cognitive benefits in healthy adults is a step the research hasn't firmly established yet, but the mechanism is biologically plausible.

Neuriva also comes in a "Plus" version that adds B6 and B12. That's a welcome addition, since B-vitamin deficiency is a common and underappreciated cause of brain fog and memory slips (see our deep dive on vitamin B12 and memory). But even the Plus version remains a narrow formula.

What does the research actually show?

Prevagen's core clinical evidence comes from a study called the Madison Memory Study, an industry-funded trial conducted by Quincy Bioscience. The study found no significant effect of apoaequorin on the primary outcome (cognitive tests) versus placebo. The company pivoted to emphasizing benefits found in a subgroup of participants who were "cognitively normal at baseline." That post-hoc analysis was criticized by independent statisticians as cherry-picking.

The Federal Trade Commission took action against Quincy Bioscience in 2017, arguing that the company's advertising claims were not substantiated. After years of litigation, a settlement was reached in 2023 that required the company to have "competent and reliable scientific evidence" for future claims. The core question about whether apoaequorin provides meaningful memory benefits remains, in the independent scientific view, unanswered.

Neuriva's evidence is more defensible, primarily because PS has a legitimate independent research base. The coffee fruit extract data is thinner but not fabricated. Neither product has been compared head-to-head in a randomized controlled trial, so the "which is better" question can only be answered at the ingredient level, not from a product trial.

How do prices and value compare?

Prevagen regular strength runs roughly $35–$45 for a 30-day supply at standard retail. Extra strength is $50–$65. Neuriva Original is typically $30–$40. Neuriva Plus, with the added B-vitamins, runs $35–$50. Both are widely available without a prescription at major pharmacy chains.

The value question matters here. For $40 a month, you could instead buy a formula with five or six well-studied ingredients at clinical doses, or you could buy just one of these limited products. If budget is a factor and you're trying to make every dollar count for brain health, that context is worth sitting with before you click "add to cart."

What real users report

Customer reviews on major retail platforms are largely positive for both products. Prevagen has tens of thousands of reviews averaging near four stars. Neuriva's numbers are similar. This is worth examining critically. In any supplement category, placebo effects are real. People who decide to invest in their brain health and pay attention to how they feel will often notice positive changes, regardless of whether the supplement itself is driving them.

Common positive reports for both products include feeling more alert, having an easier time recalling names, and feeling generally less foggy. Negative reports typically mention no noticeable effect after weeks or months of use. Neither pattern definitively confirms or refutes efficacy; real-world reviews are simply not a controlled experiment.

If you've tried one or both and felt a benefit, that's meaningful personal data. The prudent question is whether you'd feel an equal or greater benefit from a formula with more ingredients, broader mechanisms, and more independent clinical evidence.

Which is better overall?

Comparing the two head-to-head at the ingredient level, Neuriva has the more defensible scientific position. Phosphatidylserine is a genuine brain nutrient with multiple independent trials, an FDA qualified health claim, and a plausible, well-characterized mechanism. Apoaequorin's whole chain of action, from oral ingestion to blood-brain barrier crossing to cognitive effect, remains unsupported by credible independent research.

That said, recommending Neuriva over Prevagen is a bit like choosing the better of two narrow options. You can do better than either. When it comes to supplements, the evidence consistently favors formulas that combine multiple clinically studied ingredients at proper doses, targeting memory from several different angles at once.

Frequently asked questions

Which is better, Prevagen or Neuriva?

At the ingredient level, Neuriva has the stronger case: phosphatidylserine has real independent clinical backing, while apoaequorin (Prevagen's key ingredient) does not. That said, neither formula is comprehensive. Both leave out key ingredients that more complete memory supplements include at clinical doses.

Is Prevagen backed by science?

The primary evidence for Prevagen is an industry-funded study that missed its primary endpoint and found benefits only in a post-hoc subgroup analysis. The FTC challenged Prevagen's advertising claims, and a 2023 settlement required the company to hold future claims to a proper evidence standard. Independent evidence supporting apoaequorin's efficacy is essentially absent.

Does Neuriva work for memory?

Phosphatidylserine (PS), Neuriva's best-supported ingredient, has an FDA qualified health claim for reducing cognitive dysfunction risk and is backed by multiple clinical trials. At 100 mg per serving, it's at the lower end of studied doses. NeuroFactor's benefits for humans are less established. Neuriva can be a starting point for brain health support, but it's not a complete formula.
