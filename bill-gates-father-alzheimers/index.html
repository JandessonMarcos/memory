Bill Gates on His Father's Alzheimer's — and What It Taught Him

Bill Gates Sr. had Alzheimer's disease. His son has written and spoken about the experience in public — here is what he shared, what it meant for his research agenda, and what every family can take from it.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  June 24, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Bill Gates Sr. had Alzheimer's and passed away in September 2020 at age 94.
  • Gates wrote publicly about his father's illness in blog posts on GatesNotes.com and in interviews.
  • Personal motivation: watching his father's decline was a major driver of Gates's decision to fund Alzheimer's research.
  • No supplement link: Gates has never endorsed a brain pill or memory supplement — ads claiming otherwise are scams.
  • Reader takeaway: lifestyle habits, early detection, and talking to your doctor are the most evidence-based tools available today.

When Bill Gates wrote publicly about his father's Alzheimer's disease, he gave millions of people a candid window into what it is like to watch a brilliant, active parent lose memory and independence to the disease. Bill Gates Sr. — William H. Gates II — was a renowned attorney, a decorated World War II veteran, a co-founder of the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation alongside his son, and one of the most respected civic figures in Seattle. His son's accounts of his decline are among the most widely read public descriptions of Alzheimer's caregiving from anyone in the public eye. This article summarizes what Gates has shared, why it shaped his research agenda, and what practical lessons any family can draw from the experience.

Did Bill Gates Sr. have Alzheimer's disease?

Yes. William H. Gates II — widely known as Bill Gates Sr. — was publicly known to have Alzheimer's disease in his later years. He passed away on September 14, 2020, at the age of 94 at his home in Seattle, Washington. His son Bill Gates announced his death in a post on GatesNotes.com, describing him as "a pillar of strength" and reflecting on what the family had lost.

Gates Sr. remained active well into his eighties, co-chairing the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation and continuing to champion causes including estate taxes and access to justice. This makes the progressive nature of his Alzheimer's all the more striking to those who knew him. For his son and the rest of the family, the years of the disease's progression were a long and difficult caregiving experience.

What has Bill Gates written about his father's illness?

Gates has addressed his father's Alzheimer's in multiple posts on GatesNotes.com and in public interviews. In those accounts, he has described the particular cruelty of Alzheimer's: a disease that takes a person slowly, erasing memories and capabilities across months and years while the person often remains physically present for a long time. He has written about how his father changed — losing the ability to engage in the conversations and activities that once defined him — and about the emotional weight that places on a family.

Gates has also used his public platform to push back on a common misconception: that wealth and resources meaningfully change the trajectory of Alzheimer's. His family had access to excellent medical care and support, yet the disease progressed. This experience reinforced his view that what the world needs is not better ways to manage the disease's later stages, but ways to detect and treat it far earlier.

Critically: Gates has never said that any supplement, pill, or consumer product changed his father's outcome or protected his own brain. Viral online ads that use his image to promote "brain pills" are fabricated scams. See our full explainer on why there is no Bill Gates brain pill and how to spot the fraud.

How did his father's illness shape Gates's Alzheimer's research agenda?

Gates has stated directly and repeatedly that watching his father live with Alzheimer's was a primary personal motivation for investing in the disease's research. In November 2017, he announced a landmark personal commitment of $100 million to Alzheimer's research — split between the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation (ADDF) and the Dementia Discovery Fund (DDF), two organizations funding earlier-stage, higher-risk research that private industry had largely avoided.

In his public writing accompanying that announcement, Gates explained his reasoning: Alzheimer's affects tens of millions of people and their families worldwide; it has no cure; it had historically received far less research funding than diseases of comparable impact; and the window of time between first brain changes and first symptoms — potentially a decade or more — represented an opportunity that science had not yet learned to use. He saw his father's experience as evidence of how urgent the need was.

He followed the initial pledge with continued funding for the ADDF's Diagnostics Accelerator, a program aimed specifically at developing blood tests that can detect Alzheimer's years before symptoms appear. What makes Alzheimer's so hard on families?

The Gates family's experience mirrors what millions of families face, and Gates's public accounts highlight several aspects of the disease that are particularly difficult:

The slow, uneven progression: Alzheimer's does not advance in a straight line. There are periods of relative clarity followed by deeper confusion. For caregivers, this unevenness creates painful cycles of hope and loss — and makes it difficult to know what to expect or how to plan.

Identity and relationship change: A person with advancing Alzheimer's may not recognize family members, may behave in unfamiliar ways, or may lose the conversational and relational capacities that defined who they were. For adult children watching a parent change in these ways, grief often begins long before physical death.

Caregiver burden: Most Alzheimer's care is provided by family members, typically over years. The demands are physical, emotional, and financial. Research consistently shows that Alzheimer's caregivers face elevated rates of depression, anxiety, burnout, and their own health problems. The Alzheimer's Association (alz.org) offers a free 24/7 helpline and local support groups for families in this situation.

The experience of watching a parent lose their memory to Alzheimer's is one of the defining reasons Bill Gates shifted significant personal resources toward the disease — not to find a quick fix, but to build a scientific foundation that didn't yet exist.

What lessons does the Gates family's experience offer readers?

Gates has tried to channel his father's experience into urgency and action. The practical lessons he draws — and that the science supports — are worth summarizing:

Start earlier than you think you need to: Gates has consistently emphasized early detection as the highest-priority research goal. By the time a person shows Alzheimer's symptoms, the brain changes have typically been accumulating for 10 to 20 years. The earlier a diagnosis is made — ideally before symptoms appear — the more options a patient and family have. This applies to lifestyle habits as well: the brain-protective behaviors that matter most are those started years before any problems emerge.

Lifestyle factors are genuinely protective: Multiple large studies have found that regular aerobic exercise, a Mediterranean-style diet, quality sleep, avoiding tobacco, managing blood pressure and blood sugar, and staying socially and mentally active are associated with a meaningfully lower risk of Alzheimer's and cognitive decline. None of these guarantees prevention, but together they represent the best risk-reduction toolkit science currently offers. See our guide to memory-protective habits for specifics.

Nutrients can support the brain: Alongside diet and exercise, certain nutrients have meaningful research support for brain health — omega-3 DHA for brain structure and function, B-vitamins including B12 and folate for homocysteine metabolism, and compounds like citicoline and phosphatidylserine for supporting neuronal membrane function. These are not Alzheimer's treatments, but they support the underlying biology that a healthy brain needs. See our editors' picks for the best memory-support formulas of 2026.

Talk to your doctor about your family history: Having a first-degree relative with Alzheimer's roughly doubles your lifetime risk compared to the general population. This doesn't mean the disease is inevitable, but it does mean the conversation about brain health, monitoring, and lifestyle optimization should happen sooner rather than later.

Can Alzheimer's disease be prevented?

There is no guaranteed prevention. However, research strongly supports that regular exercise, a healthy diet, quality sleep, managing cardiovascular risk, and staying mentally and socially active can meaningfully reduce risk over time.
