The Memory Palace Technique: How to Build One and Use It

Ancient Greek and Roman orators used this system to deliver hours-long speeches without notes. World Memory Champions use it to memorize thousands of digits in one sitting. Here's how to build your own palace and start putting it to work.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published July 15, 2026

Key takeaways

  • This is the technique competitive memorizers use worldwide, and it works for ordinary people too.
  • You already have a palace: your home, a familiar route, any place you know without effort.
  • The core skill is visualization: turning abstract information into vivid, memorable images.
  • Most beginners see results within a few days (it's a learnable skill, not a gift).
  • It works at any age. Research confirms adults in their 60s and 70s can master it.

If you want to know how to use the memory palace technique, the short answer is this: take a place you already know, trace a route through it, and attach vivid images of whatever you need to remember to specific spots along the way. To recall the information, you mentally walk the route again. That's it. The system is two thousand years old, thoroughly studied, and still the most effective memorization method researchers have found.

What exactly is a memory palace?

A memory palace (formally called the method of loci, from the Latin word for "places") is a mental map of a familiar environment. You picture yourself walking through that environment along a fixed route, stopping at specific landmarks along the way. Each stop is a "station." You place a vivid mental image of what you want to remember at each station, then retrieve them later by retracing the walk.

The technique dates to ancient Greece. Cicero described it in his writings on rhetoric, crediting its invention to the poet Simonides of Ceos. For centuries it was the primary tool orators used to deliver long speeches without notes. The same principles apply today, whether you're trying to remember vocabulary in a new language, the steps of a complex procedure, or a list of names before a meeting.

Does the research back this up?

It does. A well-designed 2017 study published in the journal Neuron trained a group of average people in the method of loci over six weeks. Before training, they recalled about 26 words from a 72-word list on average. After training, they recalled roughly 62. More importantly, those gains persisted four months later, even without continued practice. The control groups showed no equivalent improvement.

World Memory Champions consistently score near the population average on standard cognitive assessments before they begin training. Their extraordinary recall comes from the technique, not from innate biological gifts. That's good news for the rest of us.

How do you choose a location?

Start with your current home or a childhood home you remember clearly. Familiarity is the whole point. You want a place you could walk through in your mind without any effort, so your attention is free to focus on the images you're placing rather than on figuring out where the bathroom is.

Almost any location works: a school you attended, a grandparent's house, a frequently visited park, a familiar supermarket layout. The place just needs to have distinct, memorable spots you can visit in order. As you build skill, you'll accumulate multiple palaces for different subjects.

How do you trace the route?

Write down a specific walk through your chosen location. Start at one entrance and follow a consistent path through every room or area. List 10 to 20 distinct "stations" along the way. These should be specific spots where your eyes naturally land: a coat rack, a kitchen table, a window, a mirror, a specific bookshelf.

The route must always go in the same direction. If you sometimes walk the kitchen-to-dining-room route and sometimes reverse it, you'll confuse yourself during recall. Fix the order before you start placing memories. Write it out once, memorize it, then forget the list. The route should become automatic.

How do you actually place a memory?

Here's where most people underestimate how weird the images need to be. For the technique to work, each image must be vivid, unusual, or emotionally engaging enough that your brain flags it as worth holding onto. Boring images don't stick. Absurd ones do.

Say you need to remember that the mitochondria produce ATP. At your front door, imagine a tiny power plant (the mitochondria) shooting bundles of cash labeled "ATP" directly at your face as you open the door. Strange? Yes. Hard to forget? Also yes.

Three rules for effective memory images: make them large and loud, give them movement, and physically connect them to the station. The image shouldn't be floating abstractly near the location. It should be on the coat rack, inside the mirror, crawling across the table.

What should you practice with first?

A grocery list is the ideal starting point because you can verify your recall immediately at the store. Build your palace with 8 to 10 items. At home, mentally walk the route two or three times, seeing each image clearly at its station. At the store, retrace the route instead of reaching for your phone.

Most beginners are surprised the first time this works. The images surface reliably. From there, you can move on to vocabulary lists, key facts for an exam, or the main points of a presentation. The same system scales from a grocery run to a 200-word medical glossary. You just need more stations.

How many stations do you need?

For a grocery list or a short set of talking points: 10 to 15 stations. For studying a substantial amount of material, you'll want 30 to 50 per palace. A typical house has plenty of stations if you break each room into distinct spots: doorway, left wall, center, right wall, window.

Competitive memorizers build dozens of separate palaces and assign each one to a different subject or project. That prevents confusion between separate bodies of information. Keep each palace dedicated to one topic.

How long before it feels natural?

Most people feel real confidence after three to five days of regular practice. That doesn't mean the images appear effortlessly yet. It means the system is working and you've verified that you can trust it. Actual fluency (where building and walking a palace feels as natural as taking notes) develops over a few weeks of consistent use.

One practical tip: build the palace habit before you need it for high-stakes learning. Set it up as the first step whenever you approach new material, not as a rescue tool the night before an exam. What about nutrition and brain health?

Technique matters most, but the brain's underlying hardware matters too. Memory consolidation happens during sleep, so the palace you build today gets filed into long-term storage tonight. Consistent sleep, adequate hydration, and a diet with enough omega-3s and choline all support the encoding process that makes technique-based learning stick faster.

Some people supplement to close nutritional gaps. Citicoline and bacopa monnieri are the ingredients with the most clinical data for supporting memory and learning specifically. Frequently asked questions

What is a memory palace?

A memory palace is a mental map of a familiar place. You mentally walk through it and "place" vivid images of the things you want to remember at specific spots. To retrieve them, you retrace the walk. Psychologists call this the method of loci.

Does the memory palace technique actually work?

Yes. A 2017 study in Neuron showed that six weeks of method-of-loci training brought average participants to expert-level recall, with gains maintained four months later. World Memory Champions rely on this system almost universally.

How long does it take to build a memory palace?

Choosing a location and tracing a route takes about 15 minutes. Creating your first set of vivid images adds another 20 to 30 minutes. After a few days of practice, setting up a new palace typically takes under 10 minutes.

Can anyone use the memory palace technique?

Yes. You don't need an exceptional starting memory. Just a familiar place and a willingness to think in pictures. Studies show adults of all ages, including those in their 60s and 70s, can learn the technique and see real improvements in retention.

What information is the memory palace best for?

It works best for ordered lists, sequences, vocabulary, factual information, and speeches. It's less suited to conceptual understanding or mathematical reasoning, where you need to think through problems rather than simply recall facts.

How many items can a single memory palace hold?

As many stations as you build. A typical house walk with distinct stops per room gives you 50 to 100 stations. Competitive memorizers build dozens of palaces, each holding dozens of items, for total capacities in the thousands.
