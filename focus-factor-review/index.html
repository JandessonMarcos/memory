Focus Factor Review 2026: Does It Work? An Honest Look

Focus Factor is one of the most recognized names in the brain supplement category. Here's what its label actually contains, what the research shows, and how it compares to formulas our editors rate higher.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published July 8, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Real ingredients, limited transparency: Focus Factor contains studied nootropics, but most are hidden inside a proprietary blend with undisclosed doses.
  • Multivitamin overlap: Much of the formula duplicates what a standard multivitamin already provides, at an added cost.
  • One company-sponsored trial: A modest, industry-funded study exists, but independent replication is thin.
  • Better options exist: Formulas with fully transparent, clinically-referenced labeling give you more confidence about what you're actually getting.
  • Always ask your doctor before adding any supplement to your routine, especially if you take medications.

If you've searched for brain supplements in a pharmacy or online, you've almost certainly encountered Focus Factor. It's been around for years, it's sold in major retail chains, and it markets itself as "America's #1 brain supplement." Those are bold claims. Whether the product lives up to them is a different question. Here's what's actually inside the bottle and how to think about it.

What is Focus Factor?

Focus Factor is a dietary supplement from Factor Nutrition Labs, sold as a tablet. There are two main versions: the original formula and "Focus Factor Extra Strength," which has higher doses of certain ingredients. Both versions are positioned as supporting memory, focus, and concentration.

The product has been on the market since the early 2000s, which gives it more name recognition than many newer competitors. That longevity cuts both ways: it means the brand has a real track record, but also that the formula hasn't changed much while the science around brain nutrition has moved forward.

What are the ingredients in Focus Factor?

The label divides into two sections: a standard vitamin and mineral panel, and a proprietary "Focus Factor Proprietary Blend."

The vitamin and mineral side is substantial. You get vitamins A, C, D, E, and the full B-vitamin complex, along with calcium, iron, magnesium, zinc, selenium, copper, and several others. For someone with a poor diet, that coverage is genuinely useful. For someone who already takes a daily multivitamin, it's a significant overlap.

The proprietary blend is where things get complicated. It weighs in at 640 mg total and includes a long list of ingredients:

  • DMAE (dimethylaminoethanol)
  • L-Glutamine
  • Glutamic acid
  • Bacopa monnieri extract
  • Phosphatidylserine
  • DHA (docosahexaenoic acid)
  • Huperzine A
  • Bilberry fruit extract
  • Ginkgo biloba extract
  • Vinpocetine

The problem is that "640 mg total" tells you nothing about how much of each ingredient is inside. Bacopa monnieri, for instance, is typically studied at 300 mg per day. Phosphatidylserine is studied at 100 mg three times daily. If the combined blend is 640 mg and it contains 10+ ingredients, it's mathematically impossible for most of them to be at effective research doses. That's the core issue with proprietary blends in general: they let a company list impressive-sounding ingredients without committing to effective amounts.

Does any research support Focus Factor?

Factor Nutrition Labs conducted a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial published in 2011. The study found improvements in memory, concentration, and word recall in the Focus Factor group compared to placebo. That sounds encouraging. A few caveats are worth knowing, though.

The study was industry-funded, which doesn't make it invalid but does warrant a higher level of scrutiny. The sample was modest in size. And crucially, the trial tested the full formula, so it's impossible to say which ingredients drove the effect, or whether the vitamins alone might have produced similar results in participants who were already deficient in B vitamins or vitamin D. No independent research team has published a replication of that study.

Individual ingredients in the blend do have independent research behind them. Bacopa monnieri is one of the better-studied herbal nootropics, with trials showing improvements in delayed recall after consistent use. Phosphatidylserine has an FDA-qualified health claim for cognitive decline risk reduction. Huperzine A inhibits acetylcholinesterase, an enzyme that breaks down the memory neurotransmitter acetylcholine. The question remains: are these ingredients present in Focus Factor at doses that matter?

What are the main drawbacks?

Several things stand out after a careful read of the label.

Proprietary blend dosing. This is the biggest issue. With 10+ active ingredients sharing 640 mg, most are almost certainly under the doses used in research. That's not a Focus Factor-specific problem; it's a common tactic across the supplement industry. But it does mean you're paying for ingredient names rather than guaranteed amounts.

Iron content. Focus Factor includes iron. Adult men and postmenopausal women typically don't need supplemental iron, and excess intake isn't without risk over time. This is worth noting if you fall into either group.

Daily huperzine A. Huperzine A has a relatively long half-life in the body. Some researchers suggest cycling it rather than taking it every day to avoid potential tolerance. A formula that includes it as a daily fixed dose doesn't account for this.

Price versus value. Focus Factor isn't inexpensive relative to what you get. If most of the formula is vitamins you already cover elsewhere, you're paying a premium for a small proprietary blend that may or may not be dosed effectively.

Who might consider Focus Factor?

People who don't already take a multivitamin and want a single product covering both basic nutrients and some nootropic ingredients will find more value here than someone already supplementing the basics. It's also worth noting that the formula is broadly safe for healthy adults (there are no exotic or high-risk ingredients in the primary sense).

That said, anyone with a specific goal of improving memory and focus would likely do better with a product that proves its doses on the label rather than asking you to trust a proprietary blend.

A supplement's ingredient list only tells half the story. The dose is what determines whether something can actually work — and that's what proprietary blends consistently hide.

The bottom line on Focus Factor

Focus Factor is a legitimate product with a long track record. It contains some well-studied nootropics, one industry-funded trial, and enough vitamins to serve as a multivitamin replacement for people who don't already take one. For the right person in the right situation, it could provide some benefit.

What it doesn't do is give you confidence about whether the active brain-support ingredients are present at meaningful doses. That's a real limitation. If you care about getting effective amounts of bacopa, phosphatidylserine, and citicoline in a single formula, look for products that put every milligram on the label. Start with the foundational habits that drive the most memory improvement, then layer in targeted nutrition from a formula you can actually verify.
