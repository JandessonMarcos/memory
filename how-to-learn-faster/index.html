How to Learn Faster: 7 Techniques Backed by Neuroscience

Speed in learning has almost nothing to do with talent. It comes from using methods that match how the brain stores and retrieves information. Most people never learn those methods. Here they are.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published July 21, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Test yourself, don't reread. Retrieval practice is the single most effective study method in the research literature.
  • Space your reviews. Reviewing at growing intervals beats massed cramming for long-term retention.
  • Interleave topics. Mixing related subjects in one session feels harder but produces faster, more flexible learning.
  • Protect your sleep. Memory is consolidated during deep and REM sleep. A skipped night erases much of the day's gains.
  • Focus fully. Divided attention is the quiet killer of fast learning. One task, phone away, timer running.

There's a stubborn myth that fast learners are simply born with better brains. The research tells a different story. When psychologists compare high performers to everyone else, the gap usually comes down to method, not raw wiring. Fast learners, often without realizing it, use a small set of techniques that align with how memory forms. The good news is that these techniques are learnable, and most of them are free. Below are seven that hold up under decades of cognitive science. None require a special talent. They just require doing the work in a smarter order.

1. Use active recall instead of rereading

If you take one idea from this article, make it this one. Rereading notes and highlighting a textbook feel productive. They barely move the needle. The brain strengthens a memory when it has to pull the information out, not when it passively sees it again. This is called retrieval practice, and in study after study it outperforms rereading by wide margins. A landmark 2011 experiment in Science found that students who practiced recalling material remembered about 50% more a week later than students who reread it or made concept maps. The method is simple. Close the book. Write down or say everything you can remember. Then check what you missed. That struggle to retrieve is the learning.

2. Space your practice over time

Cramming works for a Friday quiz and fails for anything you actually want to keep. The spacing effect, first documented by Hermann Ebbinghaus in the 1880s, shows that the same total study time produces far more durable memory when it's spread out. Review something today, again in two days, then in a week, then in two weeks. Each time the memory starts to fade slightly before you refresh it, the refresh makes it stronger. Apps like Anki automate this with algorithms, but you can do it with a stack of note cards and a calendar. Spacing costs you nothing extra. It just reorganizes when you study.

Retrieval and spacing work best together. Space out sessions where you test yourself, and you have combined the two most powerful tools in the entire field of learning science. Almost everything else is a refinement of these two.

3. Interleave related topics

Most people study in blocks. All of topic A, then all of topic B. Interleaving mixes them: a bit of A, then B, then C, then back to A. It feels clumsy and slower in the moment. That discomfort is the point. Mixing forces your brain to keep deciding which approach fits which problem, which is exactly the skill you need when the material shows up later out of order. Studies with math students and even medical trainees show interleaved practice produces better test performance than blocked practice, sometimes dramatically. The catch is that it feels worse while you do it, so people abandon it. Trust the research over the feeling.

4. Explain it in plain language

The Feynman technique is named after the physicist Richard Feynman, and it's deceptively simple. Pick a concept. Explain it out loud, or on paper, as if you were teaching it to a curious twelve-year-old. The moment you stumble or reach for jargon, you've found a gap in your understanding. Go back to the source, fill the gap, then try again. Teaching forces you to organize knowledge and expose the parts you only half-grasped. You don't need a real student. A wall, a pet, or a voice memo works fine. This is one reason study groups help: explaining to a peer is high-quality retrieval in disguise.

5. Guard your attention

You cannot learn fast while distracted. Attention is the doorway every memory has to pass through, and a buzzing phone slams that door every few minutes. Research on task switching shows that after an interruption, it can take many minutes to fully regain deep focus. The fix is boring but powerful. Put the phone in another room. Close every tab you don't need. Use a timer, work in a focused block of 25 to 50 minutes, then take a real break. This is often called the Pomodoro method, and its value isn't the exact numbers. It's that a running clock gives you permission to ignore everything else for a set stretch.

The biggest driver of slow learning in modern life isn't a weak brain. It's a divided one. Protect a single, undistracted block and your speed roughly doubles on its own.

6. Sleep on it, literally

Learning doesn't finish when you close the book. It finishes while you sleep. During deep sleep and REM, the brain replays and files the day's new information, moving it from fragile short-term storage into something more permanent. Skip a night and you lose a large share of what you studied. A well-known study found that people who slept after learning retained far more than those kept awake the same number of hours. This makes pulling an all-nighter one of the worst possible strategies. You trade the exact process that would have locked the material in. If you want to learn faster, treat sleep as part of the study plan, not a competitor to it. For the wider picture on rest and recall, see our guide on how to improve your memory.

7. Practice with feedback, deliberately

Hours logged matter less than what you do with them. Deliberate practice means working at the edge of your ability on the specific parts you're weakest at, then getting quick feedback and adjusting. A tennis player who serves badly should drill serves, not play more casual matches. A student who fails word problems should do word problems with the answer key close by, not reread the chapter. Research suggests that with focused, feedback-driven practice, most people can reach basic competence in a new skill in roughly 20 hours. The trap is comfortable repetition, practicing what you already do well because it feels good. Aim at the hard part instead.

Putting it together

You don't need all seven at once. Start with the two that carry the most weight: test yourself instead of rereading, and space those tests over days. Add focused, phone-free blocks and protect your sleep. Layer in interleaving, the Feynman technique, and deliberate practice as habits form. Diet, exercise, and hydration set the baseline your brain works from, so they matter too. Frequently asked questions

What is the fastest way to learn something new?

The fastest reliable method combines active recall (testing yourself instead of rereading) with spaced repetition (reviewing at growing intervals). Together they force the brain to retrieve information, which strengthens memory far more than passive review. Add focused practice sessions and enough sleep, and you learn material in a fraction of the time cramming takes.

Does learning faster mean forgetting faster?

No. The techniques that speed up learning, such as retrieval practice and spacing, are the same ones that make memories last. Cramming feels fast but fades within days. Methods that build durable memory take slightly more effort up front and save enormous time later because you don't have to relearn.

How long does it take to learn a new skill?

Research on skill acquisition suggests you can reach basic competence in most skills with roughly 20 hours of focused, deliberate practice. Mastery takes far longer. The key variable is quality of practice, not just hours logged. Concentrated, feedback-driven sessions beat distracted, repetitive ones.

Can supplements help you learn faster?

No supplement replaces good study technique or adequate sleep. Some ingredients studied for cognition, such as citicoline, caffeine paired with L-theanine, and omega-3 fatty acids, may support focus and attention in some people. Evidence is mixed and effects are modest. Treat any supplement as a minor add-on, not a shortcut, and check with your doctor first.
