Scientists Found a New Alzheimer's Trigger, and a Drug That Slows It in Mice

Researchers at ETH Zurich identified a rogue enzyme as an unrecognized driver of Alzheimer's pathology and developed a compound that blocked the cycle in mouse models. Here's what the study found and what it means for your brain health today.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published June 29, 2026

Key takeaways

  • The enzyme GRK2, when it aggregates and becomes inactive, blocks mitochondria and triggers a cycle of amyloid-beta buildup in the brain.
  • Compound 10, developed by ETH Zurich, disrupts that cycle in mouse models, improving mitochondrial function, reducing amyloid, and extending survival.
  • This is early-stage research in animals. No human trials have begun yet. Years of additional study are needed.
  • GRK2 is a new pathway not targeted by existing Alzheimer's drugs, making Compound 10 potentially complementary to current approaches.
  • The best-supported steps now remain sleep, exercise, diet, and managing stress. They address the same underlying cellular health this research targets.

Most research into Alzheimer's disease focuses on amyloid plaques and tau tangles, the two hallmarks identified decades ago. A new study from ETH Zurich, published in June 2026, points to a third driver that operates upstream of both: a cellular enzyme called GRK2 that, when it goes wrong, sets off a chain reaction damaging mitochondria and accelerating amyloid production at the same time.

The researchers also identified a chemical compound that stops the process in mice. It's early research, and the gap between mouse models and human treatments is wide. But the study opens a genuinely new angle on one of medicine's most persistent unsolved problems.

What is the new trigger they found?

GRK2 is a cellular kinase, a type of enzyme that normally helps regulate how cells respond to signals from outside. In healthy brains, it does its job and moves on. In Alzheimer's patients, ETH Zurich researchers found that GRK2 aggregates into inactive clumps that deposit directly onto mitochondria, the energy-producing structures inside nerve cells.

Those clumps block mitochondrial pores. The nerve cells are starved of energy. Under that stress, the cells produce more amyloid-beta fragments, the toxic proteins that accumulate into the plaques long associated with Alzheimer's. The amyloid-beta in turn inflicts further stress on nerve cells, which forces more functional GRK2 to inactivate and aggregate. The cycle is self-perpetuating.

This mechanism doesn't replace the amyloid and tau hypotheses; it adds to them. Researchers now have a plausible explanation for where some of the amyloid burden comes from, and a new upstream target that didn't exist on the map before.

What is Compound 10?

The ETH Zurich team screened multiple chemical agents specifically designed to prevent GRK2 from aggregating. Compound 10 was the most effective candidate they identified. In mouse models of Alzheimer's disease, treatment with Compound 10 produced three notable results: mitochondrial function improved, amyloid-beta levels fell, and the animals lived longer than untreated controls.

An unexpected finding: Compound 10 also appeared to benefit heart function and showed effects on other aging markers in the animals. Whether those additional effects would translate to humans is unknown. The compound is not a drug yet. ETH Zurich has applied for a patent on Compound 10 and is actively searching for a pharmaceutical company to take the next steps toward clinical development and, eventually, human trials.

Why does this discovery matter?

Alzheimer's drug development has a difficult history. Most experimental compounds that succeed in mice fail in human clinical trials. The disease is more complex in people, takes longer to develop, and involves multiple overlapping pathways. So caution is warranted.

That said, the GRK2 pathway is significant for a specific reason: it isn't targeted by any current Alzheimer's drug or by most experimental candidates in the pipeline. That means Compound 10, if it proves safe and effective in humans, could work alongside existing treatments rather than competing with them. A drug that targets amyloid alongside one that targets mitochondrial stress through GRK2 would be addressing the disease at two distinct points. That's a more promising design than another compound doing the same thing as the ones before it.

ETH Zurich's findings have been covered by the institution directly and via peer science reporting including ScienceDaily and Neuroscience News.

What this means for you

Compound 10 is not coming to a pharmacy anytime soon. Clinical trials take years, typically a decade or more from promising animal data to an approved drug, and the majority of Alzheimer's drug candidates don't make it through. There's no reason to expect this will be any different until human evidence exists.

What the study does represent is momentum in a field that needs it. Researchers are now attacking Alzheimer's from multiple directions at once: amyloid clearance, tau stabilization, neuroinflammation, and now mitochondrial health through GRK2. Each new validated pathway makes the overall research effort more likely to eventually yield treatments that work.

While that process unfolds over years, the protective actions available today remain the same ones the research has supported for decades. Sleep is the most evidence-backed of all. During deep sleep, the brain runs its glymphatic system, a cleaning mechanism that clears amyloid and other waste proteins from the brain overnight. Chronic sleep deprivation has been shown to accelerate amyloid accumulation. That's directly relevant to the GRK2 cycle this new research describes.

Exercise raises BDNF, supports mitochondrial health throughout the body, and has repeatedly been shown to reduce Alzheimer's risk in observational studies. A diet rich in omega-3 DHA, leafy greens, and anti-inflammatory foods gives the brain the building blocks it needs. What can support memory and brain health now?

The research-supported toolkit for protecting brain health hasn't changed with this study. Sleep consistently ranks first: the glymphatic system removes amyloid and other metabolic waste during deep sleep, and even mild chronic sleep restriction accelerates amyloid buildup over time. Consistent sleep beats catch-up nights; aim for 7–9 hours at a regular time.

Aerobic exercise matters at every age. It increases BDNF, supports hippocampal volume, and reduces systemic inflammation. Targeted nutrition can close gaps that diet alone may miss: citicoline, phosphatidylserine, omega-3 DHA, and B-vitamins are the most studied nutrients for memory and cognitive function. None of this is a treatment for Alzheimer's disease. But the same cellular health these lifestyle factors support (mitochondrial function, reduced inflammation, amyloid clearance through sleep) is exactly what the GRK2 research is targeting. The biology is connected. What you do every day interacts with the same pathways researchers are working to understand.

GRK2 is a new angle in a disease that has resisted every single approach tried so far. That makes it worth watching. What it doesn't change, yet, is the daily toolkit for protecting your brain.

Frequently asked questions

What new Alzheimer's trigger did scientists find?

ETH Zurich researchers found that the enzyme GRK2, when it aggregates and becomes inactive in the brain, blocks mitochondria, starves nerve cells of energy, and accelerates amyloid-beta production. This is a self-perpetuating cycle that existing Alzheimer's treatments don't address.

What is Compound 10?

An experimental chemical compound developed at ETH Zurich that prevents GRK2 from aggregating. In mouse studies, it improved mitochondrial function, reduced amyloid-beta accumulation, and extended survival. It is not yet available for humans.

Is Compound 10 available as a treatment?

No. This is early-stage animal research. ETH Zurich has applied for a patent and is seeking a pharmaceutical partner to begin human clinical trials. Years of additional study would be needed before any human treatment becomes available.

What can I do now to reduce my Alzheimer's risk?

The most evidence-supported steps are consistent, adequate sleep (which clears amyloid from the brain overnight via the glymphatic system), regular aerobic exercise, a brain-healthy diet rich in omega-3s and leafy greens, and managing chronic stress and cardiovascular risk factors. Always consult your doctor about your personal risk profile.
