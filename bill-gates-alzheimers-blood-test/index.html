The Early Alzheimer's Blood Test Bill Gates Helped Fund

A blood test that can detect Alzheimer's a decade before symptoms appear is becoming a clinical reality — in part because of funding Gates provided through the Alzheimer's Diagnostics Accelerator. Here's the science, what it detects, and what it means for you.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  June 24, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Breakthrough diagnostic: blood tests that detect amyloid and tau protein biomarkers can flag Alzheimer's changes in the brain years before symptoms appear.
  • Gates's role: in 2018, Gates co-funded the Alzheimer's Diagnostics Accelerator through the ADDF to fast-track blood-based Alzheimer's tests.
  • Growing clinical availability: as of 2024–2025, FDA-authorized blood-based amyloid tests are available in clinical settings for patients with cognitive symptoms.
  • Why earlier diagnosis matters: it opens a window for lifestyle intervention, emerging treatments, and clinical trial eligibility that closes once the disease is advanced.
  • Important caveat: these are physician-ordered clinical tests, not consumer products. Talk to your doctor if you have concerns about your memory.

For decades, confirming Alzheimer's disease required either a costly PET brain scan or a spinal tap — neither of which was practical at scale. A new generation of blood-based diagnostic tests is changing that, and Bill Gates is among the key figures who helped make it happen. Through the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation (ADDF), Gates co-funded an initiative to accelerate exactly these kinds of tests. This article explains his role, the science behind the tests, and what the advance means for patients and families today.

Why does Alzheimer's need a better early diagnostic test?

Alzheimer's disease begins damaging the brain silently — often ten to twenty years before the first memory problems appear. By the time a person is diagnosed based on cognitive symptoms, they may already have substantial and irreversible brain damage. This is one important reason why treatments for Alzheimer's have historically been so difficult to develop: researchers and clinicians were intervening very late in the disease process.

Earlier, more accessible diagnosis creates several concrete opportunities. It allows patients and families to plan financially, legally, and practically while the patient can still actively participate in those decisions. It opens access to clinical trials that are testing new therapies and increasingly require confirmed Alzheimer's pathology for enrollment. And as treatments that slow or interrupt the disease become available, the earlier they are started, the more brain tissue there is to protect.

This is the argument Gates has made publicly for funding diagnostic research: you cannot treat what you cannot detect. And you cannot detect it affordably until you have a practical, scalable test.

What is the Diagnostics Accelerator that Gates co-funded?

In 2018, the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation (ADDF) launched the Alzheimer's Diagnostics Accelerator — an initiative backed by approximately $30 million from Gates, Jeff Bezos, and other philanthropists. The program's explicit goal was to accelerate the development of low-cost, accessible diagnostic tests for Alzheimer's and related dementias, with priority given to blood-based approaches that could work in ordinary clinical settings.

The Accelerator funded dozens of research projects through competitive grants, supporting work on identifying and validating blood biomarkers, improving test accuracy, and navigating the regulatory pathway toward FDA authorization. Several of the research programs it supported contributed meaningfully to the scientific groundwork for tests that have since reached clinical use.

How do the new blood tests for Alzheimer's work?

Alzheimer's disease is defined pathologically by two types of abnormal protein accumulation in the brain: amyloid plaques (clumps of amyloid beta protein between neurons) and tau tangles (twisted fibers of tau protein inside neurons). These changes begin accumulating years before symptoms appear and can now be detected — at very low concentrations — in a routine blood sample.

The most clinically established blood biomarkers include:

Amyloid beta 42/40 ratio: The ratio of two subtypes of amyloid beta protein in blood reflects whether amyloid is accumulating in the brain. A lower ratio is associated with higher brain amyloid burden — an early and defining feature of Alzheimer's pathology.

Phospho-tau 217 (pTau-217): This modified form of tau in the blood has emerged as one of the most sensitive and specific blood biomarkers for Alzheimer's. Multiple large studies have found that elevated blood pTau-217 corresponds closely with amyloid and tau pathology as measured by PET brain scans. Some research suggests it becomes abnormal a decade or more before cognitive decline begins.

GFAP and NfL: Glial fibrillary acidic protein and neurofilament light chain are markers of neurodegeneration and brain injury more broadly. They are used alongside the amyloid and tau markers to provide a fuller picture of brain health.

The combination of these markers — particularly amyloid ratio and pTau-217 — allows clinicians to assess with increasing accuracy whether Alzheimer's pathology is present in the brain, often before any symptoms have appeared.

Are these Alzheimer's blood tests available today?

Yes — and access is expanding. The FDA began authorizing blood-based amyloid tests for Alzheimer's in 2024. These initial authorizations were for use in adults who are already showing cognitive symptoms and are being evaluated by a physician — not as a general population screening tool. Several laboratory platforms have received authorization, and more are expected to follow as the field develops.

Availability and insurance coverage vary. Some tests are available in academic medical centers and specialist neurology practices; others are more broadly accessible. Clinical guidelines on when and how to use these tests are still evolving, as clinicians work through questions about how to communicate and act on the results — particularly in patients who test positive before any symptoms appear.

For now, the practical guidance is: if you or a family member is experiencing memory concerns, talk to a doctor. They can evaluate what is causing the symptoms, determine whether specialist referral is appropriate, and discuss whether diagnostic testing — including blood-based tests — is right for your situation.

What does early detection mean for you and your family?

The arrival of accessible Alzheimer's blood tests has real practical implications beyond research settings. Here is how to think about what the advance means at a personal level:

If you are experiencing memory concerns: See your doctor. They can assess whether symptoms warrant specialist evaluation and whether diagnostic testing is appropriate. A full evaluation also helps identify treatable causes that mimic Alzheimer's — thyroid problems, vitamin B12 deficiency, sleep disorders, and certain medications can all impair cognition and respond well to treatment.

If a parent or family member is showing signs of cognitive decline: Encouraging early medical evaluation is the most valuable thing you can do. An early diagnosis — even a difficult one — opens the door to care planning, legal and financial preparation, and potential trial access that simply isn't available without one.

If you want to reduce your own long-term risk: The lifestyle behaviors that protect heart health also protect the brain. Regular aerobic exercise, a Mediterranean-style diet, consistent quality sleep, not smoking, and managing blood pressure and blood sugar are the interventions with the strongest evidence base. Frequently asked questions

What Alzheimer's blood test did Bill Gates help fund?

Through the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation (ADDF), Gates co-funded the Diagnostics Accelerator — launched in 2018 with approximately $30 million — to speed development of blood-based Alzheimer's diagnostic tests. Several programs it funded contributed to tests now entering clinical use.

How does an Alzheimer's blood test work?

The tests measure proteins in blood that reflect what is happening in the brain — primarily the amyloid beta 42/40 ratio and phospho-tau 217 (pTau-217). Abnormal levels of these markers indicate Alzheimer's-related pathology is present in the brain, often years before symptoms begin.

Is there an FDA-authorized blood test to detect Alzheimer's?

Yes. The FDA authorized blood-based amyloid tests beginning in 2024, for adults with cognitive symptoms being evaluated by a physician. These are clinical tests ordered by doctors. Availability varies; ask your neurologist or primary care doctor whether testing is appropriate for you.

What should I do if I'm worried about my memory?

See your doctor. They can evaluate for treatable causes, refer you to a specialist if needed, and determine whether diagnostic testing is appropriate. Early evaluation is almost always better than waiting.
