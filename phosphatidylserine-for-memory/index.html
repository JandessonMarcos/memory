Phosphatidylserine for Memory: Benefits, Dosage & Evidence

One of the few brain nutrients with a real clinical record. Here's what the research actually says, how much to take, and where it fits in a broader memory strategy.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Today

Key takeaways

  • PS is a phospholipid found in neuron membranes, where it supports cell signaling and membrane flexibility.
  • Clinical trials show modest benefits for age-associated memory impairment (AAMI), not for dementia or Alzheimer's.
  • The FDA issued a qualified health claim for PS in 2003, but with explicit language noting that evidence is limited.
  • Standard dose: 100 mg three times daily (300 mg total), taken with meals. Give it 8–12 weeks.
  • Generally safe at typical doses; check with your doctor if you take blood thinners.

Phosphatidylserine is one of the more thoroughly studied ingredients in the brain-health category. It sits inside the membranes of neurons, helping cells communicate and stay flexible. When levels drop with age, researchers have observed declines in certain memory tasks and processing speed. Whether supplementing can reverse those changes is a more complicated question, and the honest answer depends on what you're trying to address.

What exactly is phosphatidylserine?

PS is a phospholipid: a fat molecule with a charged phosphate group. It makes up roughly 15% of total brain lipid content and plays a direct role in neuron signaling, in activating protein kinase C (involved in memory formation), and in the orderly clearance of aging cells.

Your body synthesizes PS from other phospholipids, but production slows with age. Food sources include white beans, egg yolks, fatty fish, and soy, though food amounts are modest relative to doses used in trials.

Most supplements are now made from soy lecithin or sunflower lecithin. Bovine brain-derived PS was phased out following BSE (mad cow disease) concerns in the 1990s. Soy-derived PS is the most studied; sunflower-derived is a good option if you have a soy allergy or want to avoid soy entirely.

What does the clinical research say?

The research base for PS is more substantial than for many supplements in this category, though still limited enough that the FDA keeps its endorsement qualified.

In 2003, the FDA allowed a qualified health claim for PS, permitting labels to say it "may reduce the risk of dementia and cognitive dysfunction in the elderly." The operative word there is "may." The agency added explicit language noting that "the scientific evidence in support of this claim is uncertain."

Several randomized controlled trials have examined PS in older adults with age-associated memory impairment (AAMI), the normal non-clinical memory difficulties that many people notice in middle age and beyond. A number of those trials found small but statistically significant improvements in memory recall tasks, word-learning, and processing speed compared to placebo.

A review published in the Journal of Clinical Biochemistry and Nutrition examined multiple trials using soy-derived PS and reported modest improvements in specific memory metrics in adults with AAMI, with the best results at 300 mg/day over 12 weeks. Results in people with established Alzheimer's disease or more severe cognitive impairment have been far less consistent.

The honest summary: PS shows promise for the mild memory changes of normal aging. It's not a dementia treatment, and the effect sizes in trials are generally small, not dramatic. Think of it as one tool in a broader strategy, not a standalone fix.

How much phosphatidylserine should you take?

Most of the positive clinical trials used 100 mg three times daily with meals, totaling 300 mg per day. Some trials tested 200 mg once daily; results were broadly similar. Products sold at 100–200 mg per serving are standard.

PS is fat-soluble, so taking it with a meal that contains some fat may improve absorption. Timing relative to other supplements doesn't appear to matter significantly based on available evidence.

How long before you notice a difference?

Don't expect results in two weeks. The trials that found measurable improvements ran for 8 to 12 weeks. Some people report subjective changes sooner, but if you're looking for a fair test, give it at least 8 weeks of consistent daily use.

Side effects and safety

PS is well-tolerated at typical doses. The most commonly reported side effects are mild GI symptoms, such as stomach discomfort or nausea, which usually improve when taken with food.

At higher doses (above 400 mg/day), there is a theoretical concern about interactions with blood-thinning medications, since phospholipids can influence platelet activity. If you take warfarin, aspirin, or other anticoagulants, talk to your doctor before adding PS.

Soy-derived PS is typically processed in a way that removes soy proteins. Most people with soy allergies tolerate it, but if yours is severe, choose a sunflower-derived product to be safe. Pregnant or nursing women should consult their physician before supplementing.

How phosphatidylserine fits with other memory nutrients

PS works well alongside other brain-supporting ingredients. It pairs particularly well with citicoline, which raises acetylcholine and supports membrane repair, and with omega-3 DHA, which maintains the fluidity of the same cell membranes PS helps stabilize. A few trials have examined the PS-plus-DHA combination and found results somewhat more consistent than either ingredient alone.

Should you try phosphatidylserine?

If you're noticing the everyday memory slips associated with normal aging, and your sleep, diet, and exercise are already solid, PS is one of the better-evidenced options in the supplement category. The risk profile at standard doses is low, the mechanism is well understood, and the FDA has seen enough research to issue a (qualified) health claim.

It won't fix a sleep deficit or compensate for a poor diet. And you should discuss any new supplement with your doctor, especially if you take medications. But as part of a thoughtful, layered approach to brain health, phosphatidylserine earns its place in the conversation.

Frequently asked questions

What does phosphatidylserine do for memory?

PS supports neuron membrane health and cell-to-cell signaling in the brain. Clinical trials have found modest improvements in memory recall and processing speed in older adults with age-associated memory impairment, though the effect size is generally small, not transformative.

What is the best dose of phosphatidylserine for memory?

Most clinical trials used 100 mg taken three times daily with meals (300 mg total per day). Some tested 200 mg once daily with similar results. Doses below 100 mg/day are unlikely to produce measurable benefit based on current evidence.

How long does phosphatidylserine take to work?

Trials measuring cognitive outcomes ran for 8 to 12 weeks. Give it at least 8 weeks of consistent daily use before drawing conclusions about whether it's helping.

Is phosphatidylserine safe to take daily?

Generally yes, at recommended doses (100–300 mg/day). The most common side effects are mild GI symptoms. People taking blood-thinning medications should consult their doctor first, as higher doses may affect platelet activity. As with any supplement, check with your physician.
