How to Stop Forgetting Things: 9 Practical Fixes

Most everyday forgetting has a fixable cause. These nine strategies address the most common drivers, and most people notice a difference within days of fixing the first one.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published July 12, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Sleep deprivation is the most underdiagnosed cause of persistent everyday forgetfulness in adults.
  • Attention is the gateway to memory: if you didn't fully encode something, retrieval fails. That's not a memory problem; it's an attention problem.
  • External systems reduce the burden on your working memory, which has a genuinely limited capacity.
  • B12, choline, and omega-3 deficiencies can quietly impair recall for months before they're diagnosed.
  • Aerobic exercise builds new neurons in the hippocampus, the brain's memory hub.

Everyone forgets things. The question is whether it's a normal slip or a pattern worth addressing. For most people under 70, persistent forgetfulness traces to a short list of fixable causes: not enough sleep, too much on the mental plate, distracted encoding, or a nutritional gap. Before assuming the worst, try the nine fixes below.

For context on what's actually normal, our guide to memory loss vs. normal aging explains what separates everyday slips from a clinical red flag. And if you want the full picture on improving recall, see our guide on how to improve your memory.

1. Why do we forget things in the first place?

Memory has two main failure points: encoding (getting information in) and retrieval (getting it out). Most everyday forgetfulness happens at encoding. You walk into a room and forget why you went in; that's usually because you were thinking about something else when you crossed the threshold, so no memory formed. You forget where you put your keys because you set them down without paying attention. The fixes in this article target both failure points, but encoding problems are the most common and the most correctable.

2. Could sleep deprivation be the real culprit?

This one is bigger than most people realize. During deep sleep, specifically slow-wave sleep, the brain replays the day's experiences and consolidates them into long-term memory. Skip deep sleep and those memories don't stick. A study from the University of Zurich found that deep sleep quality was more predictive of next-day memory performance than total sleep time. Six hours a night has cognitive effects similar to two nights of total sleep deprivation, accumulated over weeks. Most adults need 7–9 hours, and a consistent wake time anchors the schedule as much as total duration.

3. Is chronic stress eroding your recall?

Cortisol, the primary stress hormone, directly affects the hippocampus, the brain region most responsible for forming new memories. Short bursts of stress can sharpen attention. But chronic stress (the kind that runs in the background for weeks) shrinks hippocampal volume over time and impairs memory formation. If you regularly feel mentally foggy under sustained pressure, that's likely a cortisol effect, not permanent decline. Addressing the source (exercise, better sleep, reducing workload) gradually restores normal function. Mindfulness practice has growing clinical support for reducing cortisol and improving working memory capacity.

4. Are you actually paying full attention at encoding moments?

Attention is the gateway to memory. Fully. If you don't register something consciously when it happens, there's no memory to retrieve later. This explains forgetting where you parked even though you were physically there: you walked to your car on autopilot. The fix sounds simple but takes practice. Be deliberate at encoding moments. Say a new person's name aloud right after introduction. Consciously register where you're putting something down. Put your phone away when someone is talking to you. Small acts of intentional attention at the right moment work far better than any retrieval trick applied after the fact.

5. Should you use external systems to help you remember?

Writing things down, keeping consistent routines, and using reminders isn't a sign of poor memory. It's how well-functioning working memory is supposed to operate. Your brain has limited active storage capacity, and prospective memory (remembering to do things in the future) is particularly weak compared to retrospective memory. External systems offload that burden. Keep a consistent spot for keys and wallet. Use a digital calendar with 15-minute alerts. Write tomorrow's three priorities before you go to bed tonight. Grocery lists and weekly reviews reduce the cognitive overhead of trying to hold everything in your head, freeing up working memory for things that actually require it.

6. Could a nutritional gap be quietly undermining your recall?

Three deficiencies are particularly common and often go undetected for years.

Vitamin B12 is essential for the myelin sheaths protecting nerve fibers. Deficiency causes gradual cognitive symptoms: forgetfulness, slow processing, and brain fog that can look like early dementia. It's common in adults over 50 and in anyone taking metformin long-term or following a plant-based diet. A basic blood test identifies it.

Choline (from eggs, liver, and fish) is the precursor to acetylcholine, the brain's primary memory neurotransmitter. Most adults don't meet the recommended daily intake of 425–550 mg. Consistent choline shortfall makes encoding harder over time.

Omega-3 DHA is a structural fat in brain neurons. Low intake is consistently linked to faster cognitive aging in epidemiological studies. If you don't eat fatty fish two or three times a week, you're probably low.

7. Does regular exercise actually help with forgetfulness?

Yes, and the mechanism is well understood. Aerobic exercise increases blood flow to the brain and triggers the release of BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), a protein that supports neurogenesis in the hippocampus. This matters because the hippocampus is where short-term memories get transferred to long-term storage. Even a brisk 20-minute walk produces measurable BDNF increases. Research shows that regular aerobic exercisers have larger hippocampal volumes than sedentary adults of the same age, essentially a physically larger memory center. Consistency matters far more than intensity here.

For most adults, the biggest memory upgrade isn't a brain game or a new supplement — it's seven to nine hours of sleep, done consistently, starting tonight.

8. Are there memory techniques that actually make a difference?

A few strategies have genuine research support beyond just "they feel helpful."

The method of loci involves mentally placing items you want to remember at familiar locations along a route or in a room you know well. It works by anchoring abstract information to vivid spatial memories, which the brain retains more easily. It's effective for lists and sequences.

Spaced repetition means reviewing material at increasing intervals rather than cramming. Apps like Anki automate the schedule. This is the most efficient way to move information from short-term to long-term memory and is well supported by learning science.

Active recall means testing yourself instead of re-reading. Consistently outperforms passive review in retention studies. Flashcards, practice questions, and "close the book and recite" all count.

These techniques are more valuable for deliberate learning than for everyday slips. For the latter, the sleep, attention, and external systems fixes have the higher practical impact. See our guide to memory exercises for more on training recall.

9. When does forgetfulness need a doctor's attention?

Most everyday forgetting is benign and responds to the changes above. Some patterns are different and worth bringing to a GP: forgetting conversations that happened recently (not just names or dates), getting disoriented in familiar areas, having difficulty with tasks you've done for years, or noticeable personality and judgment changes. These differ from normal slips in that they affect daily function, not just retrieval. A GP can administer a brief cognitive screen and, if needed, refer for further evaluation. The earlier that kind of assessment happens, the more options are available.

Frequently asked questions

Why do I keep forgetting things all of a sudden?

Sudden increases in forgetfulness are often tied to a change in circumstances: new stress, worse sleep, a new medication, or a nutritional shift. For most people, the cause is fixable once identified. If the change is dramatic or involves forgetting recent conversations or familiar routes, see a doctor.

Is forgetting things a sign of dementia?

Usually not. A normal slip is forgetting where you put your keys. Dementia-level loss is forgetting you own a car. Occasional forgetting is normal at any age. Consistent, worsening functional decline is different and warrants a medical evaluation. But that's not what most people searching this question are experiencing.

What vitamin deficiency causes forgetfulness?

Vitamin B12 deficiency is the most common and clinically significant one; it causes gradual cognitive symptoms, is frequently missed, and is easily corrected once identified. Low vitamin D is also consistently linked to cognitive decline. A standard blood panel identifies both.

How can I stop forgetting things at work?

The most effective strategies are: a daily written task list reviewed each morning, a consistent system for capturing incoming requests, reducing context-switching by batching similar tasks, and taking a real break at midday. Working memory has a finite capacity; reducing what you're holding in it beats trying to expand it through effort alone.
