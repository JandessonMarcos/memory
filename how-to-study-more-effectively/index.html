How to Study More Effectively: Science-Backed Methods

Most students default to re-reading their notes. It's also one of the least effective strategies researchers have found. Here's what actually works, and why the difference is larger than you'd expect.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published July 15, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Active recall beats re-reading: testing yourself on material builds far stronger memory than reading it again.
  • Spaced repetition schedules reviews for just before you'd forget, making each session more efficient.
  • Interleaving topics in one session feels harder but produces better test scores a week later.
  • 25 to 50 minutes of focused work, followed by a real break, consistently outperforms marathon sessions.
  • Sleep is when consolidation happens. Time your hardest study for the hours before bed.

If you want to know how to study more effectively, the first thing to learn is what to stop doing. Re-reading notes (the default for most students) ranks near the bottom in large reviews of study strategies. It feels productive because the material looks familiar. That feeling is the problem. Familiarity and retrievability are not the same thing, and exams test the second one.

Why does re-reading fail?

When you read a page of notes a second time, your brain notices that the content looks familiar. It interprets that as knowledge. Cognitive psychologists call this the "fluency illusion." The material feels known. But the test is whether you can produce the information without seeing it. Re-reading doesn't build that skill.

Studies comparing re-reading to other methods consistently rank it below active recall, spaced practice, and interleaving for long-term retention. Students who spend the same amount of time testing themselves remember significantly more one week and one month later, on material they've never seen on a test. The strategy gap is real.

What is active recall, and why does it work?

Active recall means retrieving information from memory without looking at your notes. You close the book, cover the page, and try to produce the answer from nothing. Flashcards are the simplest version. Practice tests are a more intensive one. Writing down everything you remember about a topic before checking your notes (sometimes called a "brain dump") is another.

Every successful retrieval strengthens the neural pathway that leads to that piece of information. The pathway becomes faster and more reliable. That's why students who test themselves during study sessions consistently outperform students who re-read, even when total study time is equal. You're not just reviewing. You're building the retrieval mechanism the exam will actually demand.

What is spaced repetition?

Spaced repetition is a scheduling approach that spaces out review sessions rather than clustering them. Instead of reviewing the same material three days in a row, you study it today, again in three days, then after a week, then after three weeks. Each review session happens just before the information would slip away.

The underlying principle goes back to Hermann Ebbinghaus and his late-19th-century research on the "forgetting curve," which describes the consistent rate at which newly learned material fades without review. Spacing the repetitions catches information at the optimal moment, which makes each session more efficient than massed practice (cramming).

Apps like Anki automate this scheduling using an algorithm. They track which items you know well and which you keep getting wrong, then adjust review intervals accordingly. For people studying large volumes of material (a language, a medical curriculum, a professional certification), spaced repetition combined with active recall is among the most powerful learning tools available.

What is interleaving?

Interleaving means mixing topics within a single study session rather than finishing one topic completely before moving to the next. Instead of spending two hours on algebra followed by two hours on geometry, you alternate between them every 20 to 30 minutes.

Performance during the session is often worse with interleaving. Students make more errors, and the material feels less settled. That's the paradox: the apparent difficulty is what produces better long-term results. Switching between topics forces the brain to figure out which concept applies to each new problem rather than pattern-matching to whatever it most recently reviewed. That extra retrieval effort builds more durable understanding.

Research consistently shows interleaved practice produces higher test scores a week and a month after study, even when session performance is identical or slightly lower. Use it primarily for material that requires distinguishing between related concepts: different problem types in math, different grammatical structures in a language, different clinical scenarios in medicine.

How long should a study session be?

Attention research points to 25 to 50 minutes of intense focus as the practical sweet spot. Beyond 90 minutes of continuous demanding work, returns diminish sharply and errors increase. Shorter sessions with genuine breaks in between consistently outperform marathon sessions of the same total duration.

The Pomodoro method (25 minutes of focused work, 5 minutes off) is widely used and aligns well with the research. Some people do better with 45 to 50 minutes on and 10 to 15 minutes off. Experiment to find your threshold. The key is that the break must be genuinely restful, meaning stepping away from the material, not checking your phone for messages about related topics.

When should you study?

Sleep is when memory consolidation actually happens. The hippocampus replays the day's learning during deep sleep, filing it into long-term storage. Studying in the two to three hours before sleep gives that material priority in the night's consolidation queue. You don't need to stay up late; you just need the last thing your brain processes before sleep to be the material you care most about retaining.

Avoid alcohol on study nights. Even moderate drinking disrupts the deep sleep stages where consolidation is most active. Does where you study matter?

Less than how you study. A quiet, dedicated study spot with minimal interruptions is better than a noisy, distracted one. The quality of the session matters more than the physical location. One finding worth knowing: studying the same material in two or three different locations slightly improves later recall, because the brain attaches the memory to multiple environmental cues rather than just one.

Phones are the real environmental factor. Research is consistent that having your phone visible (even face-down) reduces cognitive performance on complex tasks. Putting it in another room, or using a blocking app, is a small change that produces measurable gains in focus quality.

What about nutrition and supplements?

The brain runs on glucose and needs adequate hydration to maintain focus. Missing meals or letting blood sugar drop during a study session impairs concentration noticeably. Steady, real-food meals before a session matter more than any supplement.

Beyond the basics, some people find targeted nutrients useful during demanding learning periods. Citicoline and bacopa monnieri have the most human clinical trial data for supporting memory and cognitive performance specifically. Frequently asked questions

What is the most effective studying technique?

Active recall (testing yourself on material instead of re-reading it) has the strongest evidence across multiple large reviews of the research. Pair it with spaced repetition (reviewing at increasing intervals) for the best long-term results.

Does re-reading your notes help you study?

Less than most students think. Re-reading creates familiarity (the fluency illusion) without building the retrieval pathways that exams test. Switch to active recall for most of your study time and use re-reading only to fill in specific gaps you identified while self-testing.

How long should a study session be?

25 to 50 minutes of focused work followed by a real break matches the brain's attention cycle. Sessions beyond 90 minutes show sharply diminishing returns. Several shorter sessions spread across days consistently outperform one long marathon session.

What should I take to help with studying?

Start with the basics: consistent sleep, adequate hydration, and steady meals. For targeted nutritional support, citicoline, bacopa monnieri, and omega-3 DHA have the most clinical data for focus and memory. Ideally taken together in one well-dosed formula.

What is spaced repetition?

Spaced repetition schedules review sessions at increasing intervals: today, in three days, in a week, in three weeks. Each review happens just before you'd forget. This makes each session more efficient than cramming the same material repeatedly in one sitting.

Does where you study matter?

Less than how you study, but a quiet environment helps. Some research shows that studying the same material in two or three different locations slightly improves recall because the brain attaches the memory to more contextual cues.
