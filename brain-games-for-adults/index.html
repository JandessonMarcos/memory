Brain Games for Adults: Which Ones Actually Help

Not all brain games are created equal. Here's what the research actually says about which types sharpen memory and focus, and which ones mostly improve your score in that game.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published July 13, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Not all games transfer, getting good at a game mostly makes you better at that game.
  • Strategy games lead the evidence: chess, bridge, and mahjong link consistently to sharper cognition in studies.
  • Dual n-back training is one of the few digital formats with documented real-world memory transfer.
  • 15–20 minutes a day, done consistently, is enough, duration is less important than regularity.
  • Games work best alongside quality sleep, exercise and good nutrition, not as a substitute for them.

If you're searching for brain games for adults that genuinely move the needle on memory, you've probably run into conflicting claims. Some apps promise dramatic gains; many researchers say those claims are overstated. Both sides have a point. The short version: mental challenge is real, measurable brain stimulus, but the type of game matters more than most people realize, and a few specific formats do stand out.

Do brain games actually work?

The honest answer: it depends on what you mean by "work." Playing any game enough will make you better at that game. That's a genuine cognitive gain. The harder question is whether it transfers to daily life, sharper word recall, faster processing, better focus at work or in conversation.

A large 2014 consensus statement signed by more than 70 cognitive scientists concluded that evidence for broad transfer from commercial brain-training programs was weak. What the same researchers did affirm is that mental activity matters for brain health. The key distinction is between practicing a specific skill and building general cognitive capacity. Some game types bridge that gap better than others.

For a solid grounding in how the brain learns and adapts, the NIH Brain Basics guide explains neuroplasticity clearly, the same mechanism that makes certain games potentially valuable when approached correctly.

Which game types have the strongest evidence?

Strategy games sit at the top of most reviews. Chess demands sustained attention, forward planning, and working memory simultaneously, and long-term players consistently score higher on cognitive tests than non-players. Bridge and mahjong show similar patterns, partly because they add social interaction, which is its own form of cognitive exercise.

Crossword puzzles and word games rank a step below strategy games but still show meaningful benefits, especially for verbal memory and processing speed. They're easy to build into a daily routine. That regularity counts for more than it sounds.

Dual n-back training is worth separate mention. It's demanding, often frustrating, and probably the most studied brain-training task in academic literature. Several well-controlled trials found it genuinely transfers to working memory and fluid intelligence, a real exception to the "games mostly improve at that game" pattern. It won't appeal to everyone, but it has the data.

Learning-based apps (language learning being the clearest example) also perform well because they build actual new knowledge. That creates durable neural connections rather than just faster reflexes.

What about online brain-training apps?

The commercial programs vary considerably. Lumosity settled with the FTC in 2016 over unsubstantiated memory claims, and some of its exercises are closer to speed drills than genuine cognitive training. BrainHQ has more peer-reviewed support, with studies showing improvements in processing speed in older adults. Elevate focuses on language and math, a built-in advantage, since it teaches real skills.

None of them should be your entire brain-health strategy. Think of a brain-training app the way you'd think of a gym membership. The membership doesn't get you fit, showing up consistently and working hard does. Same principle applies here.

Physical games vs. digital: does it matter?

Physical and social games have one built-in advantage digital apps can't replicate: real human interaction. When you play chess in person, you're reading your opponent, managing mild competitive pressure, and holding a conversation before and after the game. Those layers activate different cognitive pathways than a solo app session on your phone.

Board games, Scrabble, Settlers of Catan, even jigsaw puzzles with a partner, share some of these properties. They haven't been studied as extensively as chess, but the combination of strategy, social contact, and focused attention checks a lot of the right boxes. How to get the most from brain training

A few habits separate people who see genuine benefit from those who plateau quickly.

Stay challenged. The moment a game becomes automatic, you've stopped training and started coasting. Bump the difficulty, vary the format, or pick something new. Novelty and ongoing challenge are the active ingredients, not any specific app or game type.

Be consistent. Research protocols typically use 15–25 minutes per day, three to five days a week. That's sufficient. Daily short sessions reliably outperform sporadic long ones.

Protect your sleep. Memory consolidation happens during deep sleep, so what you practice while awake only sticks if you sleep well after. There's no shortcut around this, our guide on sleep and memory consolidation covers the specifics.

Treat games as one part of a broader routine. Mental stimulation compounds with physical movement, social connection, and a brain-friendly diet. No single habit carries the whole load on its own.

Novelty and difficulty are the real active ingredients in brain training. The moment any challenge becomes comfortable, most of the benefit is gone.

The limits of brain training

Worth saying plainly: no brain game prevents Alzheimer's disease or other dementias. The research does not support that claim, and anyone selling a game on that premise is overstating the evidence. What consistent mental challenge can do is contribute to cognitive reserve, the brain's capacity to stay functional despite age-related changes. That's meaningful, but it's different from prevention, and the distinction matters.

If memory changes are worrying you, a game isn't the right first response. Talk to a doctor. For context on what's normal versus what's worth checking, our piece on memory loss vs. normal aging walks through the key distinctions clearly.

Putting it all together

The clearest evidence on brain health consistently points back to the same core habits: aerobic exercise, quality sleep, a Mediterranean-style diet, social engagement, and stress management. Brain games slot in as a useful complement. When diet falls short, targeted nutrients, citicoline, bacopa monnieri, and omega-3 fatty acids in particular, can fill gaps that games simply can't. Frequently asked questions

Do brain games actually improve memory?

The evidence is mixed. Games that closely match a cognitive task improve that task reliably, but broad transfer to everyday memory is limited. Structured challenges that are novel and progressively harder, strategy games, dual n-back, or language learning, show the most consistent real-world benefit.

What is the best brain game for adults?

No single game wins outright. Strategy games (chess, bridge), dual n-back training, crosswords, and learning-based apps that teach real skills all have solid evidence behind them. Variety tends to outperform sticking with one game indefinitely.

How long should you do brain training each day?

Most research protocols use 15–25 minutes per day, three to five days a week. Consistency matters more than duration. Daily short sessions appear more effective than occasional long ones.

Are online brain-training apps worth it?

Some are, some are not. Apps with progressively harder challenges and a real learning component are more credible than flashy reflex games. Consistent use adds up over time, but no app replaces exercise, sleep or a good diet.
