Free Memory Test (3 Minutes)

Two quick, science-based tasks measure your working memory and short-term recall. You get an instant score and what it means. No sign-up needed.

This is an educational self-check, not a medical or diagnostic test. It cannot diagnose dementia or any condition, and your score is affected by sleep, stress, and caffeine. If you are worried about your memory, please talk to a doctor.

What this memory test measures

This quick test uses two classic memory tasks. The first is a forward number span, a standard measure of working memory: you watch a string of digits, then type them back from memory. The sequence grows by one digit each time you succeed. Most adults land somewhere around seven, the famous "magical number seven, plus or minus two" described by psychologist George Miller. The second task is a short-term word recall: you study a list of words for a short window, then pick them out of a larger set. Together they give a rough, friendly snapshot of how your memory is performing right now.

Treat the result as a fun benchmark, not a verdict. Memory is a skill that fluctuates hour to hour. A poor night of sleep, a stressful morning, or too much coffee can all move your score. If you want to actually strengthen your memory over time, the fundamentals matter far more than any single test. Frequently asked questions

Is this memory test a medical diagnosis?

No. This is a free educational self-check, not a clinical or diagnostic tool. It cannot diagnose dementia, Alzheimer's, or any condition. Your score reflects a single moment and is affected by sleep, stress, caffeine, and distractions. If you are genuinely worried about your memory, talk to a doctor.

What is a normal number span?

The classic finding is that most adults can hold about 7 items in working memory, give or take 2, so a forward number span of 5 to 9 is typical. Spans naturally vary between people and dip when you are tired or distracted. A lower score on one attempt is not a cause for concern by itself.

How can I improve my memory score?

The strongest levers are the unglamorous ones: consistent sleep, regular aerobic exercise, managing blood pressure and blood sugar, and staying mentally engaged. Techniques like active recall, spaced repetition, and chunking also help you use the memory you have. Practice on a single test mostly improves that test, so focus on the fundamentals.

Does the test store my results or personal data?

The test runs entirely in your browser and does not store your score unless you choose to enter your email to receive tips. You can take it as many times as you like.
