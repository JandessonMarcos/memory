Ginkgo Biloba for Memory: Does It Really Work?

One of the most tested herbal supplements in the world has a more complicated track record than the marketing suggests. Here's what the research actually shows.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Today

Key takeaways

  • Active compounds: flavonoids and terpenoids (ginkgolides, bilobalide) that may improve cerebral blood flow and reduce oxidative stress.
  • Short-term evidence: modest improvements in cognitive speed and some memory tasks, especially in older adults with age-related complaints.
  • Long-term evidence: the large GEM trial found ginkgo did NOT prevent Alzheimer's or slow cognitive decline vs. placebo over 6 years.
  • Standard dose: 120–240 mg/day of standardized extract (24% flavonoids, 6% terpene lactones).
  • Safety flag: may increase bleeding risk when combined with blood thinners; check with your doctor.

Ginkgo biloba comes from one of the oldest tree species still living. Its leaf extract has been used in traditional medicine for centuries, and since the 1990s it became one of the top-selling herbal supplements in the United States and Europe. The promise on the label: sharper memory, clearer thinking, and possibly some protection against Alzheimer's. Understanding exactly where the evidence holds up, and where it doesn't, is worth doing before you spend money on it.

What is in ginkgo biloba extract?

Ginkgo leaf extract contains two main classes of active compounds. Flavonoids, including quercetin and kaempferol, supply antioxidant activity. Terpenoids, specifically ginkgolides and bilobalide, are thought to inhibit platelet-activating factor and may improve circulation in small blood vessels.

The extract studied in most modern clinical trials is a standardized product called EGb 761, often marketed under the brand Tebonin in Germany and Europe where it's licensed as a prescription drug. Standardized to 24% flavone glycosides and 6% terpene lactones, it gives researchers a consistent product to test. Not all supplements on store shelves match this specification, which is one reason trial results don't always translate cleanly to commercial products.

What does the research actually show?

The picture is genuinely mixed, and it matters whether you're looking at short trials or long ones.

Shorter trials, typically 8 to 16 weeks, have found modest improvements in specific cognitive tests. Speed of information processing, timed reaction tasks, and some word-recall measures showed small but statistically significant improvements in several studies, particularly in adults who reported age-associated memory difficulties. A 2012 Cochrane review noted "consistent positive effects on cognitive speed" across these shorter studies, while also flagging that results were not uniform across all outcomes or all populations.

The most definitive long-term trial, however, pointed in the other direction. The GEM (Ginkgo Evaluation of Memory) study, published in JAMA in 2008, enrolled 3,069 community-dwelling adults aged 75 and older. Researchers randomized participants to 240 mg/day of EGb 761 or placebo and followed them for a median of 6.1 years. The result was clear: ginkgo biloba did not reduce the incidence of dementia or Alzheimer's disease. It also did not slow cognitive decline in people who started with normal cognition or with mild cognitive impairment.

That's a hard result to dismiss. A rigorous, well-powered, long-term trial using a standardized extract at a meaningful dose found no protective effect against dementia. Ginkgo biloba is not a dementia preventative.

What dose and what form should you look for?

If you decide to try ginkgo, use a standardized extract. Look for a product that specifies at least 24% flavone glycosides and 6% terpene lactones on the label. Unstandardized ginkgo products are difficult to evaluate and have not been used in the major clinical trials.

The doses studied in those trials range from 120 mg to 240 mg per day, typically taken as two doses with meals. Less than 120 mg/day is unlikely to produce measurable benefit, based on what the pharmacokinetic data suggests about active compound levels.

Give it at least 8 weeks. The shorter trials that found cognitive benefits ran for 8 to 16 weeks. Judging in less than 8 weeks is premature.

Is ginkgo biloba safe?

For most healthy adults at standard doses, yes. The main concern is its mild anticoagulant effect. Ginkgolides inhibit platelet-activating factor, which can affect blood clotting. At typical doses the effect is small, but if you take warfarin, clopidogrel, aspirin at high doses, or other blood thinners, the combination may increase bleeding risk. Talk to your doctor before adding ginkgo to any anticoagulant regimen.

Other reported side effects in clinical trials include mild headaches, nausea, and dizziness, generally at rates close to placebo. Ginkgo is not recommended during pregnancy, as its effects on fetal development are not well studied.

One historical safety note: unprocessed raw ginkgo seeds are toxic and must not be eaten. Only the processed leaf extract is used in supplements and has the safety record referenced here.

How does ginkgo compare to other memory supplements?

For the specific goal of memory and focus support, ginkgo biloba is probably not the first ingredient to prioritize based on current evidence. Ingredients like citicoline and phosphatidylserine have more consistent trial records for memory-relevant outcomes, and neither carries a major long-term null result from a well-powered RCT.

That said, ginkgo is inexpensive, appears safe for most people, and the shorter-term evidence for cognitive speed is at least consistent. Some people use it as one part of a broader regimen. The biggest trial of ginkgo biloba found it did not prevent Alzheimer's over six years. Shorter studies suggest modest benefits for age-related memory complaints. Not the same thing.

Who might benefit from ginkgo biloba?

Given what the evidence shows, the most realistic use case is for older adults who notice mild, age-associated memory difficulties and are looking for a low-cost, low-risk addition to a broader brain-health strategy. Not as a dementia treatment. Not as a replacement for the fundamentals.

Those fundamentals matter more than any supplement. Sleep quality, regular physical activity, and a diet rich in whole foods have far stronger evidence for protecting long-term brain health than ginkgo or most other supplements. If you do try ginkgo, buy a standardized product, use the right dose, give it a real trial period, and track whether it's helping. And as with any supplement, discuss it with your doctor, particularly if you have underlying health conditions or take medications.
