Inside the Diagnostics Accelerator Bill Gates Co-Founded

In 2019, Gates joined forces with Jeff Bezos and other philanthropists to fund a program with one specific goal: develop affordable blood tests that can detect Alzheimer's years before symptoms appear. Here's how it works and why it matters.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Today

Key takeaways

  • Launched 2019: the Diagnostics Accelerator was announced at the Alzheimer's Association International Conference with roughly US$30 million in initial funding.
  • Run by the ADDF: the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation administers the grant program and selects research teams.
  • Three tracks: blood-based tests, digital/wearable biomarkers, and alternative fluid tests (saliva, urine) are all in scope.
  • The gap it fills: today's gold-standard Alzheimer's tests require brain PET scans or spinal taps. Both are expensive and hard to access widely.
  • Why it matters to you: early detection is the gateway to better treatments, better trials, and more time to act.

Most people do not get an Alzheimer's diagnosis until they are already experiencing memory problems. By that point, the disease has typically been active in the brain for 15 years or more. Getting a definitive diagnosis today still requires either an expensive brain scan (costing thousands of dollars) or a lumbar puncture. That barrier is part of why so many drug trials fail and why so many patients miss the window when intervention could do the most good.

The Alzheimer's Diagnostics Accelerator is a grant program built specifically to change that. Bill Gates co-funded it in 2019, alongside Jeff Bezos, Leonard Lauder, and the Dolby family, with the aim of accelerating cheap, accessible tests that could work in a standard clinical lab. Understanding what it does helps explain why Gates sees early detection as the central bet in the fight against Alzheimer's.

What exactly is the Diagnostics Accelerator?

The Diagnostics Accelerator (DA) is a competitive grant program administered by the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation (ADDF). It funds research teams working on practical, scalable biomarker tests for Alzheimer's. The emphasis is on tests that are affordable enough to be used widely, not just in specialist research centers.

It launched at the 2019 Alzheimer's Association International Conference in Los Angeles. The initial funding package was reported at roughly US$30 million, with Gates, Bezos, and several other individual donors providing the capital. The ADDF manages disbursements, reviews applications and oversees the scientific output. As covered by the Alzheimer's Association at launch, the program's explicit goal was to compress the timeline for diagnostic tools that could be used in regular clinical practice.

Who co-founded it, and why did they get involved?

Gates had been publicly interested in Alzheimer's diagnostics since at least 2018, when he wrote on Gates Notes about what he saw as the diagnostic gap at the heart of the disease problem. The argument is straightforward: without a reliable and affordable early test, you cannot run the right clinical trials, you cannot intervene early, and you cannot protect people in the window when protection might matter most.

Bezos had a separate personal interest in aging research through his company Altos Labs and other ventures. Lauder, chairman of the Estee Lauder Companies and a longtime philanthropy figure, and the Dolby family also contributed. The group shared a view that the tools for detection were lagging badly behind the broader research effort, and that private capital could move faster than traditional grant cycles. Gates had already invested US$50 million in the Dementia Discovery Fund in 2017. This was the next logical step.

What kinds of tests is it actually funding?

The program covers three broad approaches to Alzheimer's detection. The first and largest is blood-based biomarker tests. Researchers are measuring levels of proteins in the bloodstream linked to the earliest stages of Alzheimer's, particularly amyloid-beta fragments and phosphorylated tau. When these proteins are abnormal, it can indicate disease activity years before any cognitive symptom appears.

The second track is digital and wearable biomarkers. Some research groups are exploring whether subtle changes in gait, sleep patterns, reaction time or eye movement can flag early cognitive changes. Wearable devices and smartphone-based tasks could potentially screen large populations without a blood draw at all.

Third are alternative fluid tests using saliva or urine. These are further from clinical use, but the appeal is obvious: a home-based test with no needle would dramatically lower the access barrier for regular screening.

Why does early detection change everything?

This is the real heart of the issue. Alzheimer's drug trials have a dismal success record. Most major pharmaceutical candidates have failed in late-stage testing. Researchers have increasingly identified a likely reason: by the time trial participants enrolled, damage to the brain was too extensive for any drug to reverse.

If you can screen people in their late 40s or 50s, while biological signals are present but symptoms are not, you can enroll them in trials at the stage where a drug might actually prevent damage rather than attempt to repair it. You also give individuals more time. Lifestyle changes have better outcomes when adopted earlier; so do any medications that eventually reach approval.

Early detection also benefits the healthcare system. Dementia costs the US economy an estimated hundreds of billions of dollars annually, mostly in long-term care. Catching and potentially slowing the disease earlier compresses the window of severe impairment and reduces that burden. Gates has cited this economic argument alongside the personal one.

How much progress has been made since 2019?

Meaningful progress, though the work is ongoing. Blood-based tests measuring phosphorylated tau and amyloid-beta are now being used in some clinical settings and have received regulatory attention in the US and elsewhere. These tests are not yet universal standard of care, but their accuracy has improved significantly since 2019, and several are in advanced clinical validation stages.

The digital biomarker track is less mature but moving. Some research groups have shown that smartphone-based cognitive assessments can detect subtle changes with reasonable sensitivity. Wearable monitoring for sleep disruption (itself an early Alzheimer's signal) is also an active area.

None of this constitutes a solved problem. The tests that exist today are better than nothing but not yet where the field wants them to be. The Diagnostics Accelerator's job is to keep funding teams who are closing that gap.

What does this mean for your brain health right now?

For most people, the practical upshot is this: the diagnostic revolution is coming, but it hasn't arrived yet in routine primary care. In the meantime, what you do every day still matters enormously.

The lifestyle factors most clearly linked to lower dementia risk are the same ones most doctors have been recommending for general health: aerobic exercise several times a week, consistent sleep, a diet high in vegetables and oily fish and low in processed food, blood pressure control, limited alcohol and staying mentally and socially engaged. Frequently asked questions

What is the Alzheimer's Diagnostics Accelerator?

The Diagnostics Accelerator is a grant program launched in 2019 and run by the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation (ADDF). It was funded with roughly US$30 million from Bill Gates, Jeff Bezos, Leonard Lauder, the Dolby family, and other philanthropists. Its goal is to speed up development of affordable, simple biomarker tests (particularly blood-based tests) that can detect Alzheimer's years before symptoms appear.

Who co-founded the Diagnostics Accelerator?

Bill Gates announced the Diagnostics Accelerator alongside Jeff Bezos, Leonard Lauder, the Dolby family, and others at the 2019 Alzheimer's Association International Conference. The program is administered by the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation (ADDF).

What kinds of tests is the Diagnostics Accelerator developing?

The program funds three types of detection approaches: blood-based biomarker tests (measuring proteins like amyloid-beta and phosphorylated tau in the bloodstream), digital and wearable biomarkers (using devices to detect early cognitive changes), and non-blood fluid tests such as saliva- or urine-based assays. The goal is to replace expensive brain PET scans and spinal taps with tests a regular lab can run.

Why does early Alzheimer's detection matter so much?

Alzheimer's biological changes begin accumulating in the brain roughly 15 to 20 years before the first memory symptoms appear. By the time most people are diagnosed today, extensive damage has already occurred. Early detection creates a window for lifestyle intervention, trial enrollment at the right disease stage, and eventually for drugs to act before too much is lost. Without that window, even promising treatments have no realistic chance of working.
