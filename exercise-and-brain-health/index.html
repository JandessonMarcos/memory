Exercise and Brain Health: How Movement Protects Memory

Of all the things you can do to protect your memory as you age, regular aerobic exercise may be the most powerful. Here's what decades of research reveal about the brain-body connection.

By Sarah Coleman, Health Editor  ·  Reviewed by Dr. Marcus Reed, MD  ·  Published July 11, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Aerobic exercise has the strongest evidence for brain health of any lifestyle habit studied.
  • BDNF, a protein that promotes neuron growth, rises significantly with regular exercise.
  • The hippocampus (the brain's memory hub) can actually increase in volume with sustained aerobic activity.
  • 150 minutes per week of moderate movement is the most commonly recommended target for brain benefits.
  • It's never too late. Adults in their 70s show measurable brain improvements from starting a fitness routine.

If you want to do one thing for your memory, exercise is probably it. The evidence connecting regular physical activity to better brain health is deep, replicated across hundreds of studies, and spans everything from sharper short-term recall to reduced dementia risk decades later. What does exercise actually do to the brain?

When you raise your heart rate through aerobic activity, a cascade of biological changes follows in the brain. Blood flow increases, delivering more oxygen and glucose to neurons. The body releases a protein called BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), sometimes described informally as "fertilizer for the brain." BDNF supports the survival of existing neurons and the growth of new ones, particularly in the hippocampus.

The hippocampus is the brain region most directly linked to memory formation. A well-cited 2011 study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences found that older adults who walked briskly three times per week showed a 2% increase in hippocampal volume over one year, while sedentary controls experienced the typical 1.4% decline that comes with age. That's a meaningful reversal of a trend that most people assume is inevitable.

Exercise also reduces chronic inflammation, which is increasingly recognized as a driver of cognitive decline. And it improves insulin sensitivity, which matters because insulin resistance in the brain is linked to impaired memory and higher Alzheimer's risk. The benefits compound. They don't come from a single mechanism.

Which type of exercise works best?

Aerobic activity (anything that raises your heart rate and keeps it elevated) has the most consistent evidence. Walking, jogging, cycling, swimming, dancing. The specific activity matters less than the cardiovascular demand. You want to be breathing harder than normal but still able to hold a conversation. That's roughly the moderate-intensity zone where most of the research is anchored.

Resistance training also contributes, particularly for executive function (planning, decision-making, mental flexibility). Several studies suggest that combining aerobic and resistance training produces better overall cognitive outcomes than either alone. Think of a 20-minute walk followed by some bodyweight exercises as a more complete brain workout than either activity on its own.

High-intensity interval training (HIIT) has been studied too, and it does spike BDNF acutely. But the research base is smaller, and it's not accessible to many older adults. For most people, consistency matters far more than intensity. A 30-minute brisk walk five days a week beats an occasional intense session every time.

How much exercise is enough?

The most commonly cited target from the clinical literature is 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week. That's 30 minutes on five days, or slightly more on fewer days. Studies using this level of activity consistently report improvements in memory, processing speed, and attention.

Shorter sessions still help. Research has found that a single 20-minute aerobic workout can sharpen focus and memory retrieval for several hours afterward, likely through an acute BDNF spike. So even on weeks when life gets in the way, brief sessions add up. Don't let the perfect schedule be the enemy of a good walk.

For people who are currently sedentary, starting small is fine. A 10-minute daily walk is a better starting point than nothing, and you can build from there. What matters is that you get moving and stay consistent over weeks and months, because the structural brain changes take time to develop.

How quickly will you notice a difference?

Faster than most people expect. Memory retrieval and attention tend to sharpen within a few weeks of starting a regular exercise routine. Mood often improves within days, which indirectly helps cognition because stress and low mood both impair recall.

Deeper structural changes, like hippocampal growth and increased gray matter density, develop over months. That's why studies typically run for 6–12 months when they're measuring brain volume changes. But you don't need to wait months to feel the benefits. Short-term wins tend to come early and motivate continued effort.

If you're also dealing with stress-related cognitive symptoms, exercise is particularly worth prioritizing. Chronic stress shrinks the hippocampus over time; aerobic exercise counteracts this directly. For more on that link, see our piece on how stress affects memory and how to reverse it.

Does exercise actually reduce dementia risk?

Observational evidence is consistent: physically active people develop dementia at substantially lower rates than sedentary people. A 2019 review of 38 studies found that regular aerobic exercise was associated with a roughly 45% reduction in dementia risk over a lifetime, though that figure comes from observational data, not randomized trials, so causality is not established with certainty.

What researchers do know is that exercise addresses several biological risk factors for dementia: it reduces inflammation, improves vascular health, lowers insulin resistance, and supports neuron survival. It's not a guarantee. But it's one of the highest-leverage things you can do, particularly when started well before any symptoms appear.

Importantly, it's not too late to start at any age. Studies have enrolled adults in their 60s and 70s with no prior exercise habit and found significant cognitive improvements within 6 months. The brain responds to exercise throughout life. That's one of the most reassuring findings in all of cognitive neuroscience.

Exercise alongside sleep, diet, and supplements

Exercise works best as part of a broader brain-health approach rather than in isolation. Sleep is when the brain consolidates memories formed during the day, and good sleep quality amplifies the BDNF effects of exercise. Deep sleep and memory consolidation are closely linked.

Diet adds another layer. A brain-healthy diet rich in omega-3s, leafy greens, and antioxidants supports the vascular health that aerobic exercise is also building. These inputs compound rather than cancel each other out.

A 30-minute brisk walk, done consistently five days a week, produces more measurable brain benefits than most people realize. The dose is modest. The return is not.

A simple place to start

If you're not currently exercising, you don't need a gym membership or a training plan. Start with walking. Thirty minutes at a pace that feels mildly challenging, on most days of the week. Add resistance exercises when that feels easy. Keep going. The effects on your memory, mood, and long-term brain health are real and well-documented. Talk to your doctor before starting any new exercise routine, particularly if you have cardiovascular or musculoskeletal conditions.

Exercise is the cheapest and most evidence-backed brain investment available. It takes time and consistency, but it works.

Frequently asked questions

What type of exercise is best for brain health?

Aerobic exercise (walking, jogging, cycling, swimming) has the strongest evidence. Resistance training helps executive function. A mix of both produces the best overall results.

How much exercise do you need to improve memory?

About 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week (roughly 30 minutes on five days) is the level most commonly studied and shown to produce brain benefits. Even 20-minute sessions help.

How quickly does exercise improve brain function?

Focus and memory retrieval can sharpen within a few weeks. Structural brain changes, like hippocampal growth, take months of consistent effort to develop.

Can exercise reduce the risk of dementia?

Observational evidence strongly suggests yes. Active people develop dementia at lower rates, and exercise targets several key biological risk factors. It's not a guarantee, but it's one of the most modifiable protective factors available.

Is it too late to start exercising for brain health at 60 or 70?

No. Studies show measurable cognitive improvements in adults in their 60s and 70s who start regular exercise programs. The brain remains responsive to aerobic activity throughout life.
